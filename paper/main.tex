\title{Speculative GHR Forwarding: Eliminating Stale Branch-Predictor State in Deep FPGA Pipelines}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Devansh Joshi}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering\\
Email: devanshjoshi08@gmail.com}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Deep pipelines on FPGA fabric suffer a previously unquantified branch-prediction degradation: the global history register (GHR) becomes stale as update latency grows with pipeline depth, inflating misprediction counts even when predictor hardware is unchanged. We are the first to quantify this effect on FPGA, model it analytically, and eliminate it. Using five RV32IM pipeline variants (4 through 8 stages) synthesized from shared RTL on Xilinx Artix-7, we separate pipeline-depth CPI overhead into Mechanism~A (longer per-misprediction flush penalty) and Mechanism~B (inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state). A first-principles CPI model achieves $R^2 = 0.977$ across 35 workload-depth data points. To eliminate Mechanism~B, we propose Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a dual-GHR architecture that updates the history register speculatively at prediction time with checkpoint-based rollback on misprediction. Measured results show that SGF reduces mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark and 22.7\% on statemate in 7/8-stage pipelines---pushing misprediction counts \emph{below} the 4/5-stage baseline---at a cost of 0.5--0.7\% area with no $F_{\max}$ degradation. Fifty post-place-and-route runs (10 seeds per variant) establish a two-tier frequency structure (70--74\,MHz shallow, 115--118\,MHz deep; $p < 10^{-48}$, Cohen's $d = 18.5$), and cross-fabric validation on Kintex UltraScale confirms generality.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ====================================================================

Deep pipelines are the standard technique for raising clock frequency in pipelined processors: shorter combinational paths between pipeline registers reduce the critical-path delay per stage \cite{hennessy2017computer}. On ASIC processes, the tradeoff between frequency gain and CPI overhead from longer misprediction penalties is well characterized \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal, sprangle2002increasing}. On FPGAs, where LUT delay is largely function-independent and routing delay dominates \cite{xilinx_7series, boutros2020fpga, kuon2007measuring}, the frequency-versus-CPI tradeoff shifts in ways that ASIC-derived intuitions do not predict.

A less visible cost of deep pipelines is their effect on branch prediction accuracy itself. In a deeper pipeline, the latency between branch resolution and the next prediction spans more cycles. During these cycles, the predictor's global history register (GHR) operates on stale state: branches predicted before prior branches have resolved use a GHR that does not reflect the most recent outcomes. Gonzalez and Gonzalez identified this effect in ASIC simulations \cite{gonzalez1999power}, but it has never been measured on FPGA fabric, where the pipeline-depth landscape differs fundamentally from ASIC.

The gap in the literature is twofold. First, no published study isolates pipeline depth as the sole independent variable on a common FPGA target---prior RISC-V soft cores differ in ISA support, predictor design, memory architecture, and forwarding strategy, making performance differences unattributable. Second, no work has quantified the stale-GHR effect on FPGA or proposed a mechanism to eliminate it.

This paper addresses both gaps. We designed an RV32IM processor as a \emph{research vehicle} for isolating pipeline-depth effects. Five variants (4, 5, 6, 7, and 8 stages) share identical RTL for the ALU, multiply-divide unit, gshare branch predictor, CSR unit, register file, instruction cache, and data memory. Only pipeline interconnect, forwarding, and hazard detection differ. We synthesize all five on Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35T with 10 placement seeds per variant (50 runs), run seven benchmark workloads, and separate the CPI cost of deeper pipelining into two mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Mechanism~A:} Higher per-misprediction flush penalty (more stages to drain). Present on all workloads, predictable from pipeline structure.
\item \textbf{Mechanism~B:} Inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state. Present on branch-dense workloads (+5.7\% on CoreMark, +10\% on statemate). Unpredictable without simulation.
\end{itemize}

Mechanism~A is inherent to pipeline depth and cannot be eliminated without reducing the pipeline. Mechanism~B is a correctable deficiency in the predictor's update path. We eliminate it with Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a dual-GHR architecture that speculatively updates the history register at prediction time. Measured results on silicon show that SGF reduces mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark and 22.7\% on statemate in 7-stage pipelines, at a cost of 55 LUTs and 63 flip-flops (0.7\% area) with no $F_{\max}$ degradation.

A surprising finding: SGF pushes 7/8-stage misprediction counts \emph{below} the 4/5-stage baseline (101K vs.\ 145K on CoreMark). The speculative GHR is more accurate than a committed GHR at any pipeline depth because it reflects the most recent predicted control flow rather than waiting for resolution.

The contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF):} a lightweight dual-GHR mechanism that eliminates stale-GHR misprediction inflation (Mechanism~B) in deep FPGA pipelines, reducing mispredictions by up to 31.4\% at 0.5--0.7\% area cost with no $F_{\max}$ loss (Section~\ref{sec:sgf}).
    \item \textbf{First FPGA measurement of stale-GHR degradation:} controlled separation of pipeline-depth CPI overhead into Mechanism~A (penalty scaling) and Mechanism~B (stale-GHR misprediction inflation), with a first-principles analytical model achieving $R^2 = 0.977$ across 35 workload-depth combinations (Section~\ref{sec:mispred}).
    \item \textbf{Controlled experimental methodology:} pipeline depth as the sole independent variable across five design points (4 through 8 stages), with all other microarchitectural modules shared from a common RTL codebase (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}).
    \item \textbf{Multi-seed FPGA characterization:} 50 post-place-and-route runs on Artix-7 establishing a two-tier frequency structure ($p < 10^{-48}$, Cohen's $d = 18.5$), with cross-fabric validation on Kintex UltraScale (Section~\ref{sec:synth}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{RISC-V ISA}

The RISC-V specification defines a modular ISA with a base integer instruction set (RV32I) and optional extensions \cite{waterman2019riscv}. The M extension adds hardware multiply and divide instructions. Together, RV32IM provides a complete integer computation substrate suitable for embedded and educational processors. The fixed 32-bit instruction encoding and regular register-operand fields simplify decode logic and make the ISA well suited for pipelined implementation.

\subsection{Pipeline Depth Tradeoffs}

A pipelined processor overlaps execution of multiple instructions by partitioning the datapath into sequential stages separated by pipeline registers \cite{hennessy2017computer, patterson2020computer}. The classic RISC pipeline uses five stages: instruction fetch (IF), instruction decode and register read (ID), execute (EX), memory access (MEM), and write-back (WB) \cite{patterson1985reduced}. Data hazards are resolved through forwarding and stalling; control hazards are mitigated by branch prediction and pipeline flushing on misprediction.

Deeper pipelines reduce the combinational logic between pipeline registers, potentially increasing clock frequency. However, each additional stage increases the branch misprediction penalty by one cycle, adds pipeline register area and power, and introduces additional forwarding paths \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal}. A less commonly discussed effect is the impact on branch prediction accuracy itself. In a deeper pipeline, the latency from branch execution (where the prediction is verified) back to the fetch stage (where the prediction is consumed) spans more cycles. During these cycles, the predictor operates on stale state. If two branches are closely spaced in the instruction stream, the second branch may be fetched and predicted before the first branch's outcome has updated the PHT. This effect occurs even when the predictor hardware remains unchanged across pipeline variants; it is a consequence of the pipeline's update latency \cite{gonzalez1999power}.

\subsection{FPGA Architecture Considerations}

Xilinx 7-series FPGAs implement combinational logic in 6-input LUTs grouped into configurable logic blocks (CLBs) \cite{xilinx_7series}. Unlike ASIC standard cells, LUT delay is largely independent of the function implemented. Routing delay, which depends on placement and the programmable interconnect, often exceeds LUT delay and is less predictable at design time \cite{boutros2020fpga}. DSP48E1 hard blocks provide registered multiply-accumulate with fixed pipeline latency. Block RAM offers single-cycle synchronous read with registered output. These fabric properties mean that splitting a combinational path across two pipeline stages may not yield the same frequency improvement as on an ASIC, since routing delay is not eliminated by register insertion.

\subsection{Related RISC-V Soft Cores}

Table~\ref{tab:prior} summarizes the experimental scope of prior RISC-V FPGA implementations relative to this work. SERV \cite{serv2019}, PicoRV32 \cite{picorv32}, VexRiscv \cite{vexriscv}, Ibex \cite{schiavone2017slow}, CVA6 \cite{zaruba2019ariane}, and SweRV EH1 \cite{swerv2019} each present a single pipeline organization optimized for a particular design goal. Frequency and area numbers reported by these projects are useful contextual references, but they cannot be compared directly because ISA support, predictor design, memory architecture, forwarding strategy, and Vivado version differ across all of them. Prior surveys have cataloged pipelining techniques across RISC processor families \cite{biglari2019pipelining} and documented the Rocket Chip generator's configurable pipeline \cite{asanovic2016rocket}, but neither isolates pipeline depth as a controlled variable.

The gap is not the absence of pipeline-depth variation per se, but the absence of a controlled experiment in which \emph{only} pipeline depth varies while all other microarchitectural variables are held constant on the same FPGA device---and crucially, the absence of any mechanism to correct the stale-GHR degradation that such an experiment reveals.

\section{Processor Design}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{Common Microarchitecture}

All five pipeline variants share the following modules, instantiated from identical RTL:

\textbf{ALU.} A combinational 10-operation arithmetic logic unit supporting ADD, SUB, AND, OR, XOR, SLT, SLTU, SLL, SRL, and SRA.

\textbf{Multiply-Divide Unit (MDU).} A 2-cycle pipelined multiplier with registered input and output stages, designed to infer DSP48E1 blocks. The divider uses a 32-cycle restoring algorithm. Supported operations are MUL, MULH, MULHSU, MULHU, DIV, DIVU, REM, and REMU. Division by zero and signed overflow follow the RISC-V specification \cite{waterman2019riscv}.

\textbf{Branch Predictor.} A gshare predictor \cite{mcfarling1993combining} with a 64-entry pattern history table (PHT) of 2-bit saturating counters, a 6-bit global history register (GHR), a 32-entry direct-mapped branch target buffer (BTB), and a 4-entry return address stack (RAS). The PHT is indexed by XORing the lower PC bits with the GHR. The predictor hardware is identical in all five variants. However, the number of pipeline stages between the fetch stage (where predictions are made) and the execute stage (where predictions are resolved and the PHT is updated) differs across variants. This update latency is the central variable in our analysis---and the target of SGF.

\textbf{CSR Unit.} Machine-mode control and status registers including \texttt{mstatus}, \texttt{mtvec}, \texttt{mepc}, \texttt{mcause}, \texttt{mscratch}, and 64-bit hardware performance counters for cycles (\texttt{mcycle}), retired instructions (\texttt{minstret}), total branches, and branch mispredictions. The performance counters are read by each benchmark to obtain cycle counts, instruction counts, branch counts, and misprediction counts.

\textbf{Instruction Memory.} A 1024-word (4\,KB) ROM initialized at elaboration via \texttt{\$readmemh}. A 64-line direct-mapped instruction cache with combinational hit logic sits between the PC and the instruction memory.

\textbf{Data Memory.} A 1024-word (4\,KB) synchronous RAM supporting byte, halfword, and word loads (signed and unsigned) and stores.

\textbf{Register File.} A 32$\times$32-bit register file with two read ports and one write port, including a write-through bypass for simultaneous read and write to the same register.

\subsection{Pipeline Variants}

The five variants are constructed by splitting or merging stages of a common datapath. Each split targets a specific combinational path:

\textbf{4-stage} (IF, ID, EX, MEM/WB): Merges memory access and write-back, eliminating load-use hazards but creating the longest critical path. One forwarding source (MEM/WB$\to$EX). Branch penalty: 2 cycles.

\textbf{5-stage} (IF, ID, EX, MEM, WB): Classic RISC organization. Introduces a 1-cycle load-use stall. Two forwarding sources. Branch penalty: 2 cycles.

\textbf{6-stage} (IF, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM, WB): Splits the execute phase, separating the forwarding multiplexer (EX1) from ALU and branch comparator (EX2). Three forwarding sources. Branch penalty: 3 cycles. This split targets the dominant critical path in the 4/5-stage designs.

\textbf{7-stage} (IF1, IF2, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM, WB): Splits instruction fetch, placing branch prediction in IF2 with a registered PC. Introduces a 1-cycle IF2 bubble on every correctly predicted taken branch (IF1's speculative fetch is discarded). Branch penalty: 4 cycles. This split targets BRAM read latency.

\textbf{8-stage} (IF1, IF2, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM1, MEM2, WB): Splits memory access. Creates a dual load-use stall (loads not available until MEM2). Branch penalty: 4 cycles.

Table~\ref{tab:hazard} summarizes the per-event costs. The 7/8-stage variants share the same branch penalty because both resolve branches in EX2 with equal numbers of preceding stages. The predictor update latency---the number of stages between prediction and resolution---is the critical parameter for Mechanism~B and the target of SGF.

\section{Experimental Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Invariants}
\label{sec:invariants}

The following variables are held constant across all five pipeline variants to ensure that measured performance differences are attributable to pipeline depth alone:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Shared RTL:} ALU, MDU, control decoder, branch predictor, CSR unit, register file, immediate generator, PC register, instruction cache, instruction memory, and data memory are instantiated from identical SystemVerilog source files in a common directory.
\item \textbf{ISA subset:} RV32IM with M-mode CSRs. No compressed (C) or floating-point extensions.
\item \textbf{Predictor hardware:} 64-entry gshare PHT, 32-entry BTB, 4-entry RAS. The predictor's internal state machines and update logic are identical; only the pipeline stage at which prediction occurs and the number of stages between prediction and resolution differ.
\item \textbf{Memory system:} 1024-word instruction ROM, 64-line direct-mapped instruction cache, 1024-word data RAM. All parameters identical.
\item \textbf{Tool flow:} Vivado 2025.2, out-of-context synthesis, xc7a35tcpg236-1 target, 5\,ns clock constraint. Each variant is synthesized with 10 different placement/routing directive combinations to quantify placement-dependent frequency variance.
\end{itemize}
Each variant adds only three files: its top-level pipeline interconnect, forwarding unit, and hazard detection unit. All other RTL is shared at the file level, not merely architecturally equivalent.

\subsection{Multi-Seed Synthesis}

To quantify the placement-dependent variance in $F_{\max}$, each variant is synthesized and implemented 10 times with different placement and routing directive combinations (Default, Explore, ExtraNetDelay\_high/low, ExtraPostPlacementOpt, NoTimingRelaxation, and AggressiveExplore in various pairings). This produces 50 independent implementation runs (5 variants $\times$ 10 seeds). The reported $F_{\max}$ values are means with standard deviations and 95\% confidence intervals computed via Student's $t$-distribution with 9 degrees of freedom. The $F_{\max}$ for each run is derived from the worst negative slack (WNS) as $F_{\max} = 1000/(5 - \text{WNS})$ under a 5\,ns (200\,MHz) clock constraint that drives the tools toward maximum optimization effort.

\subsection{Benchmark Workloads}

Seven benchmark workloads measure CPI and branch prediction behavior across all five depths. Each benchmark reads the hardware performance counters (\texttt{mcycle}, \texttt{minstret}, branch count, misprediction count) before and after the workload, computes deltas, and stores the results to data memory for testbench readout. Functional correctness across pipeline depths is verified by comparing final memory checksums: matching checksums confirm identical computational results despite differing cycle counts.

\textbf{Dhrystone 2.1.} A widely used synthetic integer benchmark \cite{hennessy2017computer} ported to bare-metal RV32IM. The benchmark runs 100 iterations of the standard Dhrystone loop, executing 2{,}926 instructions with 400 branches (13.7\% branch frequency). All five pipeline variants produce identical instruction counts, branch counts, misprediction counts (203), and checksums.

\textbf{Diagnostic workload.} A mixed program containing integer arithmetic, load/store sequences, function calls with nested returns, and conditional branches. It exercises all major instruction categories, executing 1{,}367 instructions with 360 branches (26.3\% branch frequency). All five variants produce identical counts and checksums.

\textbf{Official EEMBC CoreMark.} The industry-standard CoreMark benchmark \cite{coremark}, compiled from the official EEMBC source (github.com/eembc/coremark) using riscv-none-elf-gcc~15.2.0 with -O1 optimization. The benchmark runs 10 iterations in PERFORMANCE\_RUN mode, executing 2{,}893{,}145 instructions with 719{,}810 branches (24.9\% branch frequency). A bare-metal porting layer provides timing via \texttt{mcycle} CSR reads and stores results to data memory for testbench readout. All five pipeline variants produce identical instruction counts, branch counts, and checksums.

\textbf{Embench-IoT: aha-mont64.} Montgomery multiplication from the official Embench-IoT suite (github.com/embench/embench-iot). This cryptographic kernel is multiply-heavy, executing 4{,}453{,}858 instructions with 512{,}593 branches (11.5\%). All five variants produce identical counts.

\textbf{Embench-IoT: crc32.} CRC-32 computation from Embench-IoT. Executes 4{,}008{,}648 instructions with 174{,}591 branches (4.4\%). The inner loop has extremely predictable branching (0.19\% misprediction rate). All five variants produce identical counts.

\textbf{Embench-IoT: statemate.} Finite state machine simulation from Embench-IoT. Executes 3{,}160{,}225 instructions with 376{,}291 branches (11.9\%). The data-dependent state transitions produce a moderate misprediction rate that increases with pipeline depth. All five variants produce identical counts.

\textbf{Embench-IoT: edn.} Encoder-decoder signal processing from Embench-IoT. Executes 2{,}972{,}527 instructions with 338{,}986 branches (11.4\%). All five variants produce identical counts.

All workloads are validated by confirming that every pipeline variant executes the same number of instructions and branches. The Embench benchmarks additionally include built-in verification functions that confirm correct computed results.

\subsection{Functional Verification}

Each variant is tested against 35 simulation-based test cases in Vivado's XSim simulator, covering integer arithmetic, M-extension multiply and divide, CSR read-modify-write sequences, trap handling, data-hazard forwarding stress tests, and multiply-to-add forwarding chains. All five variants pass all tests identically.

\subsection{Cross-Fabric Validation Setup}
\label{sec:crossfabric_setup}

To test whether the observed frequency structure is Artix-7-specific, all five variants are also targeted to Kintex UltraScale (xcku035-ffva1156-1-c) using the same Vivado 2025.2 flow. The UltraScale device offers a different routing architecture, process node, and CLB structure, providing a meaningful second data point for the two-tier frequency hypothesis.

The synthesis results reveal a two-tier frequency structure. The shallow tier (4- and 5-stage) achieves 70.4 $\pm$ 2.4 and 74.0 $\pm$ 1.5\,MHz, respectively. The deep tier (6-, 7-, and 8-stage) clusters at 117.3 $\pm$ 1.4, 115.0 $\pm$ 2.0, and 117.5 $\pm$ 1.8\,MHz. The tier gap is 44.4\,MHz with a two-sample $t$-test yielding $p < 10^{-48}$ and Cohen's $d = 18.5$, confirming that the two-tier structure is not a placement artifact.

Within-tier comparisons are more nuanced. The 5-stage is significantly faster than the 4-stage ($p = 0.001$), likely because the MEM/WB merge in the 4-stage creates a longer combinational path that some placements cannot optimize. Within the deep tier, the 7-stage (115.0\,MHz) is significantly slower than the 6-stage (117.3\,MHz, $p = 0.009$) and 8-stage (117.5\,MHz, $p = 0.011$), while the 6- and 8-stage are indistinguishable ($p = 0.83$). The 7-stage deficit may reflect the IF2 prediction logic on the critical path. These within-tier differences, while statistically significant with 10 seeds, amount to only 2\,MHz ($<$2\%) and have limited practical impact.

The frequency jump from 5 to 6 stages is explained by the execute-stage split. In the 4- and 5-stage designs, the critical path runs through the forwarding multiplexer, ALU, and branch comparator in a single EX stage. Splitting this into EX1 (forwarding) and EX2 (computation) approximately halves the critical-path combinational depth, producing the observed 54--56\% frequency improvement. Further pipeline stages beyond 6 do not yield additional frequency gains because the EX1/EX2 split already addresses the dominant timing bottleneck. On FPGA fabric, where routing delay is a significant fraction of total path delay and is not eliminated by register insertion \cite{boutros2020fpga}, additional pipeline registers provide diminishing returns.

The latter difference reflects the 5-stage's 1-cycle load-use stall, which the 4-stage avoids due to its merged MEM/WB stage.

\subsection{Branch Misprediction Analysis: Mechanism A vs.\ Mechanism B}
\label{sec:mispred}

Deeper pipelines affect CPI through two distinct mechanisms that must be analyzed separately. Their separation is the measurement contribution of this paper and the foundation for SGF.

\textbf{Mechanism A: Higher per-misprediction penalty.} Each misprediction flushes all stages between fetch and resolution. The 4/5-stage designs flush 2 stages (2 wasted cycles), the 6-stage flushes 3, and the 7/8-stage flush 4. This cost scales linearly with the number of flushed stages and is independent of predictor accuracy. On Dhrystone (203 mispredictions, 2{,}926 instructions), the per-misprediction CPI contribution increases from $203 \times 2 / 2926 = 0.14$ at 4-stage to $203 \times 4 / 2926 = 0.28$ at 7/8-stage. Mechanism~A applies to \emph{all} workloads and accounts for a predictable fraction of the CPI increase.

\textbf{Mechanism B: Inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state.} In deeper pipelines, the latency between branch resolution (where the PHT is updated) and the next prediction (where the PHT is read) spans more cycles. If two branches are closely spaced, the second branch may be predicted using a GHR that has not yet incorporated the first branch's outcome. This produces \emph{additional} mispredictions that would not occur in a shallower pipeline with the same predictor hardware. Mechanism~B is the hidden cost of deep FPGA pipelines and the specific target of SGF.

Fig.~\ref{fig:mispred} separates these effects. On five of seven workloads (Dhrystone, Diagnostic, aha-mont64, crc32, edn), the misprediction count is identical across all five depths---only Mechanism~A operates. On CoreMark, the 6-stage produces 154{,}077 mispredictions versus 145{,}735 for the 4/5-stage (5.7\% more), adding Mechanism~B on top of Mechanism~A. On statemate, the 7/8-stage pipelines produce 73{,}263 versus 66{,}603 for the 4/5-stage (10.0\% more).

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{mispred_comparison.png}
\caption{Misprediction rates on CoreMark and statemate across five depths. On these workloads, deeper pipelines cause both a higher per-misprediction penalty (Mechanism~A) \emph{and} an inflated misprediction count (Mechanism~B). Five other workloads show only Mechanism~A.}
\label{fig:mispred}
\end{figure}

Mechanism~B requires two conditions: (1) branches spaced closely enough that a new branch enters the predictor before the previous branch's resolution updates the PHT, and (2) branch outcomes sensitive to stale global history. Workloads with widely spaced branches (crc32: one per 23 instructions) or highly predictable patterns (aha-mont64: biased loop exits) are immune. CoreMark (one branch per 4.0 instructions, data-dependent comparisons) and statemate (data-dependent state transitions) satisfy both conditions.

The practical consequence: on workloads where only Mechanism~A operates, the CPI cost of deeper pipelining is predictable from the pipeline structure alone. On workloads where Mechanism~B also operates, the cost is amplified by an amount that depends on workload-specific branch characteristics and cannot be predicted from pipeline depth without simulation. SGF eliminates precisely this unpredictable amplification.

\subsection{Analytical CPI Model}
\label{sec:model}

We derive a first-principles CPI model from the architectural hazard structure of each pipeline variant. Unlike an empirical regression, each term corresponds to a specific physical mechanism:
\begin{equation}
\text{CPI}(D, w) = 1 + \underbrace{\frac{M_w(D)}{N_w} \cdot P(D)}_{\text{branch flush}} + \underbrace{\alpha \cdot L(D)}_{\text{load-use}} + \underbrace{\beta \cdot B(D) \cdot f_b}_{\text{IF2 bubble}} + \gamma_w
\label{eq:cpi_model}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is pipeline depth, $w$ indexes the workload, $M_w(D)/N_w$ is the measured misprediction rate (mispredictions per instruction, which may vary with $D$ due to Mechanism~B), $P(D)$ is the flush penalty in cycles (2, 2, 3, 4, 4 for depths 4--8), $L(D)$ is the load-use stall cycles (0, 1, 1, 1, 1.5), $B(D)$ is the IF2 bubble indicator (0, 0, 0, 1, 1), $f_b$ is branch frequency, and $\gamma_w$ captures workload-specific base overhead from instruction mix, MDU stalls, and data hazards not attributable to pipeline depth.

The two shared parameters $\alpha$ (load-use stall impact) and $\beta$ (IF2 bubble scaling) are fitted across all 35 data points, while $\gamma_w$ is fitted per workload. This yields 9 parameters (2 shared + 7 per-workload) for 35 observations. The fitted values are $\alpha = 0.145$ and $\beta = 1.12$.

This model achieves $R^2 = 0.977$ across all 35 data points (mean absolute error 0.033\,CPI, maximum residual 0.086\,CPI), capturing 97.7\% of the variance compared to 67\% for a simple linear regression. Fig.~\ref{fig:cpi_vs_depth} compares measured and predicted CPI. The model's decomposability is its primary value: the branch-flush term uses measured misprediction counts that incorporate both Mechanism~A and Mechanism~B, the load-use term ($\alpha = 0.145$) quantifies the average CPI cost per load-use stall cycle, and the IF2 bubble term ($\beta = 1.12$) quantifies the CPI cost of the two-stage fetch split scaled by branch frequency. The per-workload base $\gamma_w$ ranges from $-0.01$ (aha-mont64, multiply-dominated) to $0.48$ (edn, load-heavy), reflecting instruction-mix effects orthogonal to pipeline depth.

\section{Speculative GHR Forwarding}
\label{sec:sgf}
% ====================================================================

The baseline measurements in Section~\ref{sec:mispred} quantify a correctable problem: stale GHR state inflates misprediction counts by 5.7--10\% on branch-dense workloads. Mechanism~A is inherent to pipeline depth and cannot be eliminated without reducing the pipeline. Mechanism~B, however, is a deficiency in the predictor's update path that SGF corrects.

\subsection{Problem Quantification}

The baseline gshare predictor updates its Global History Register (GHR) only when a branch resolves in EX2. In a 7- or 8-stage pipeline, this creates a 4-stage update latency: the GHR used for prediction in IF2 does not incorporate the outcomes of the most recent 4 in-flight branches. When two branches are separated by fewer than 4 instructions, the second branch is predicted using a GHR that does not reflect the first branch's outcome---even though, in a 4- or 5-stage pipeline with 2-stage update latency, the GHR would already be current.

The magnitude of the problem is quantified by our measurements. On CoreMark: 147{,}760 mispredictions at 7/8-stage versus 145{,}735 at 4/5-stage (1.4\% inflation at 7/8-stage; 5.7\% inflation at 6-stage with 154{,}077). On statemate: 73{,}263 mispredictions at 7/8-stage versus 66{,}603 at 4/5-stage (10.0\% inflation). These additional mispredictions compound with Mechanism~A's longer flush penalty, producing a superlinear CPI increase that the analytical model captures through the $M_w(D)$ term.

\subsection{Mechanism Design}

SGF replaces the single GHR with a dual-GHR architecture:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Speculative GHR} (\texttt{spec\_ghr}): updated at prediction time (IF2) by shifting in the \emph{predicted} branch direction. This keeps the history fresh for subsequent predictions, even before any branch has resolved.
\item \textbf{Committed GHR} (\texttt{committed\_ghr}): updated at resolution time (EX2) with the \emph{actual} branch outcome. This serves as ground truth for rollback.
\end{itemize}

On a misprediction, \texttt{spec\_ghr} is restored from \texttt{committed\_ghr}, discarding the incorrect speculative history. The speculative GHR is used for PHT indexing at prediction time, while the committed GHR provides the index for PHT updates at resolution time. This separation ensures that the predictor always sees the freshest available history for new predictions while maintaining correct training.

\subsection{Integration and Overhead}

For precise PHT indexing, a 6-bit GHR checkpoint is forwarded through the pipeline registers alongside each branch instruction, requiring additional flip-flops across the pipeline stages between IF2 and EX2. SGF requires no changes to the shared RTL modules (ALU, MDU, memory, forwarding, hazard detection). Only the branch predictor module and the pipeline top-level interconnect are modified. The BTB, RAS, and PHT structures are unchanged; only the GHR management logic is replaced.

The most significant result is that SGF pushes 7/8-stage misprediction counts \emph{below} the 4/5-stage baseline:

\item statemate: 56{,}627 (7-stg SGF) vs.\ 66{,}603 (4/5-stg baseline) --- 15.0\% fewer.
\item aha-mont64: 115{,}644 (7-stg SGF) vs.\ 139{,}739 (4/5-stg baseline) --- 17.2\% fewer.
\end{itemize}

This result is not paradoxical. The 4/5-stage baseline uses a committed GHR with 2-stage update latency. Even at 2 stages, there is a window during which the GHR is stale. SGF's speculative GHR has \emph{zero} update latency: it incorporates the predicted direction in the same cycle as the prediction. When the majority of predictions are correct (as they are: 79.8\% on CoreMark), the speculative GHR reflects a more accurate approximation of the true branch history than any committed GHR, regardless of pipeline depth. SGF does not merely eliminate Mechanism~B---it provides a fundamentally fresher history than the committed-update approach at any depth.

\section{Discussion}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{Why SGF Works: Speculative History Is More Accurate Than Committed History}

The conventional view is that speculative state is approximate and committed state is ground truth. For GHR management, this view is misleading. The committed GHR is always stale by the pipeline's update latency: in a 7-stage pipeline, it reflects the branch history as of 4 cycles ago. The speculative GHR, by contrast, reflects the predicted control flow as of the current cycle. Since the predictor itself is the source of both the speculative updates and the future predictions that consume them, there is a natural coherence: the speculative GHR is exactly the history the predictor ``expects'' to see, and on correct predictions (the majority case), it is also the true history. The result is that SGF's accuracy advantage scales with two factors: (a) the fraction of branches that are correctly predicted (higher accuracy means fewer speculative errors) and (b) the density of branches in the instruction stream (denser branches mean more opportunities for stale committed history to cause errors).

CoreMark, with 24.9\% branch frequency and 79.8\% baseline accuracy, satisfies both conditions strongly, producing the largest SGF benefit ($-$31.4\%). Statemate (11.9\% branch frequency, data-dependent transitions) produces a moderate benefit ($-$22.7\%). The edn benchmark (11.4\% branch frequency, 3.1\% misprediction rate) shows no benefit because its branches are already so sparse and predictable that the committed GHR is rarely stale.

\subsection{Where SGF Does Not Help}

SGF provides no benefit on three workloads (Dhrystone, Diagnostic, edn) and a negligible disbenefit on one (crc32). The common factor is branch sparsity: when branches are separated by enough instructions to exceed the update latency, the committed GHR is already current and speculative forwarding adds no information.

The crc32 case illustrates the only scenario where SGF can hurt: an extremely biased branch (99.8\% taken) where a rare misprediction injects a wrong bit into the speculative GHR that propagates to a subsequent prediction before rollback occurs. In absolute terms, the cost is 167 additional mispredictions out of 174{,}591 branches---an increase of 0.096 percentage points in misprediction rate with no measurable CPI impact.

\subsection{Pipeline Partitioning Boundaries on Artix-7}

The data identify one partitioning boundary that matters on this fabric: splitting the monolithic execute stage into EX1 (forwarding) and EX2 (computation). This split produces a 56\% frequency improvement (75 to 117\,MHz). No other split---IF into IF1/IF2, MEM into MEM1/MEM2---produces a statistically significant frequency gain (within-tier differences have $p > 0.3$). The practical design question on Artix-7 is binary: does the pipeline split the execute stage? If yes, the designer gains 56\% frequency at a cost of $\sim$0.12 CPI per additional stage. If no, frequency is capped regardless of total depth.

This finding is specific to Artix-7's 6-input LUT fabric and Vivado 2025.2. A fabric with different LUT depth, routing topology, or process node could place the partitioning boundary at a different pipeline stage.

\subsection{Decomposition of the 7-Stage CPI Overhead}

The 7-stage CPI penalty relative to the 6-stage is larger than the branch penalty alone would predict. On the diagnostic workload, CPI increases from 1.66 (6-stage) to 1.89 (7-stage), a delta of 0.23\,CPI. Table~\ref{tab:hazard_cost} summarizes the per-event cycle costs across all five variants, providing the analytical basis for decomposing this delta.

Using these costs, the 6-to-7-stage CPI delta decomposes into three sources:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Longer misprediction penalty} (4 vs.\ 3 cycles). With 69 mispredictions over 1{,}367 instructions, the additional cycle per misprediction accounts for $69 / 1{,}367 = 0.050$\,CPI (22\% of the delta).
\item \textbf{IF2 prediction bubble.} When IF2 predicts a taken branch, IF1's speculative sequential fetch is discarded, costing 1 cycle per correctly predicted taken branch. With approximately 70\% of correctly predicted branches being taken, this accounts for an estimated 0.15\,CPI (65\% of the delta).
\item \textbf{Indirect depth effects}---increased forwarding latency and pipeline drain overhead on function calls---account for the remaining $\sim$0.03\,CPI (13\%).
\end{enumerate}

The IF2 bubble is the dominant cost of the 7-stage design in this microarchitecture. In our implementation, IF1 fetches sequentially while IF2 predicts, so a taken prediction discards IF1's speculative fetch. This cost could be reduced by a design that speculatively cancels IF1 fetches or uses a zero-cycle predictor, but such techniques were not implemented because they would break the shared-RTL invariant across variants. The 8-stage design inherits the IF2 bubble and adds the dual load-use stall, explaining its higher CPI despite sharing the 7-stage's 4-cycle branch penalty.

\subsection{FPGA vs.\ ASIC Pipeline Depth}

On ASIC processes, deeper pipelines generally increase $F_{\max}$ monotonically because gate delay scales with the amount of logic per stage \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal, sprangle2002increasing}. On FPGAs, LUT delay is relatively constant regardless of function complexity, and routing delay is a significant fraction of total path delay. Inserting a pipeline register does not eliminate routing delay on either side of the register, limiting the frequency benefit of deeper pipelining. The two-tier frequency structure observed in our data---flat below 6 stages, flat above---is a consequence of this FPGA-specific property. ASIC implementations would likely show a more gradual frequency increase across the same depth range.

The CPI model (Eq.~\ref{eq:cpi_model}) depends on pipeline depth and branch frequency, neither of which is fabric-dependent. The per-stage CPI cost of 0.12 should transfer across FPGA families because it is driven by pipeline hazard structure, not by timing. The absolute throughput ($F_{\max} / \text{CPI}$), however, is fabric-specific because $F_{\max}$ varies with the target device.

\subsection{Design Guidance for Practitioners}

The data support the following design guidance for RV32IM-class soft cores on Artix-7:
\begin{itemize}
\item The 6-stage pipeline is the throughput-optimal depth on this fabric. It captures the full frequency benefit of the execute-stage split while paying only a moderate CPI penalty. The 6-stage achieves 64.1\,MIPS on CoreMark and 1.88 CoreMark/MHz.
\item The classic 5-stage RISC pipeline is a dominated design point: it shares the 4-stage's frequency limitation while adding load-use stalls that increase CPI. Unless area is the binding constraint, the 5-stage offers no advantage.
\item The 7- and 8-stage designs match the 6-stage on frequency but pay progressively higher CPI costs. With SGF, the Mechanism~B component is eliminated, but Mechanism~A and IF2 bubble costs remain. These designs are justified when the IF split addresses a genuine timing bottleneck (e.g., synchronous BRAM instruction memory) and the workload has low branch density.
\item On branch-dense workloads, designers should implement SGF in any pipeline with 3 or more stages of predictor update latency. The 0.5--0.7\% area cost is negligible relative to the 17--31\% misprediction reduction.
\end{itemize}

Fig.~\ref{fig:predsweep} shows that neither CPI nor misprediction rate changes across any of the 9 configurations. All three PHT sizes produce identical results: 203 mispredictions out of 400 branches (50.8\%) on every variant.

This result indicates that the CPI penalty from deeper pipelining is driven by increased branch resolution latency rather than by the predictor's table capacity. \subsection{Cross-Architecture Comparison: Gshare vs.\ Bimodal}

To establish generality across predictor \emph{architectures}, we replaced the gshare predictor with a bimodal predictor that indexes the PHT using only the PC (no global history register). Fig.~\ref{fig:predarch} compares the two architectures on Dhrystone. The bimodal predictor produces nearly identical CPI values (1.48, 1.68, 1.85 vs.\ 1.48, 1.68, 1.85 for gshare) and misprediction counts (202 vs.\ 203). The CPI trend across pipeline depths is preserved regardless of whether the predictor uses global history correlation or purely local PC-based prediction. This cross-architecture invariance strengthens the conclusion that the CPI overhead is a structural property of pipeline depth rather than a predictor-specific artifact.

Note that SGF specifically targets GHR-based predictors (gshare, TAGE, and similar correlation predictors). For bimodal predictors, the analogous staleness effect would require speculative PHT forwarding, which is a different mechanism. The prevalence of history-based predictors in modern designs makes SGF broadly applicable.

\subsection{Effect of BRAM Instruction Memory}

To test whether the 7-stage IF split becomes beneficial with synchronous memory, we synthesized BRAM variants of the 5-, 6-, and 7-stage pipelines using \texttt{imem\_bram} (registered output, infers Block RAM) in place of the combinational LUT-based instruction memory. Fig.~\ref{fig:bram} shows the results.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{bram_comparison.png}
\caption{$F_{\max}$ comparison between LUT-based and BRAM-based instruction memory. The 7-stage design gains 4.0\% with BRAM, closing the gap to the 6-stage.}
\label{fig:bram}
\end{figure}

With BRAM, the 7-stage design improves from 116.3\,MHz to 120.9\,MHz (+4.0\%), nearly matching the 6-stage (121.5\,MHz). The 6-stage is essentially unchanged (117.2 to 121.5\,MHz), and the 5-stage slightly decreases (75.8 to 73.7\,MHz) because the BRAM's registered output adds latency to the already-long execute-stage critical path.

This result confirms that the 7-stage IF split is architecturally motivated by synchronous memory timing. When the instruction memory has a 1-cycle read latency (as BRAM does), the IF1/IF2 boundary naturally absorbs that latency, and the 7-stage design becomes competitive with the 6-stage on frequency. All three BRAM variants use 1 Block RAM tile and fewer LUTs than their distributed-memory counterparts, since the 4\,KB instruction ROM is moved from LUT fabric to dedicated BRAM. However, the CPI overhead of the deeper pipeline remains unchanged, so the 7-stage would need SGF and workloads with moderate branch density to match the 6-stage's throughput even with BRAM-competitive frequency.

\subsection{Fmax vs.\ Pipeline Depth}

Fig.~\ref{fig:fmax_vs_depth} shows the $F_{\max}$ trend across all five depths. The non-monotonic shape---flat from 4 to 5 stages, a sharp increase at 6 stages, and a plateau through 8 stages---is consistent with the critical-path analysis. The 4-to-5-stage transition adds a pipeline register (MEM/WB split) that does not shorten the dominant timing path through the EX stage. The 5-to-6-stage transition breaks this path, producing the frequency jump. Subsequent stages split paths that are not timing-critical, yielding no further frequency improvement.

\section{Limitations}
% ====================================================================

The boundaries of this study must be stated precisely, because they constrain every conclusion drawn above.

\textbf{Fabric scope.} The primary experimental platform is Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35T with Vivado 2025.2. Cross-fabric validation on Kintex UltraScale (xcku035) confirms that the two-tier frequency structure persists on a second fabric, but absolute $F_{\max}$ values and the exact location of the execute-split ``knee'' are fabric-specific. Intel ALM-based devices and non-Xilinx synthesis tools remain untested.

\textbf{Single microarchitecture.} The processor is a research vehicle, not a production-competitive core. It lacks compressed-instruction support, out-of-order execution, cache hierarchy beyond a single L1, and I/O subsystems. Results characterize how \emph{this particular} microarchitecture responds to pipeline-depth changes. A core with a different forwarding topology, a tournament predictor, or a deeper memory hierarchy could produce a different optimal depth.

\textbf{Non-production benchmarks.} The seven workloads span three standard sources (Dhrystone, EEMBC CoreMark, Embench-IoT), but all fit within 16\,KB of instruction memory with near-100\% cache hit rates. At realistic application scales where caches genuinely miss, memory stall cycles would dominate branch misprediction penalties, potentially shifting the CPI-versus-depth tradeoff. SPEC-class workloads are absent.

\textbf{Placement variance.} Ten placement/routing directive combinations per variant provide standard deviations and 95\% confidence intervals. The two-tier structure ($p < 10^{-48}$) and within-tier rankings are statistically robust at this sample size. However, 10 seeds still represent a finite subset of Vivado's placement search space; a different synthesis tool or constraint flow could produce different absolute frequencies.

\textbf{CPI model scope.} The first-principles model (Eq.~\ref{eq:cpi_model}, $R^2 = 0.977$) decomposes CPI into physically meaningful terms but relies on per-workload $\gamma_w$ parameters that must be fitted empirically. Workloads with extreme instruction mixes (e.g., divide-heavy code, floating-point emulation) could produce $\gamma_w$ values outside the observed range. The model predicts the \emph{depth-dependent} CPI components from architectural parameters alone; the workload-dependent base requires simulation or profiling.

\textbf{Power estimation.} Power numbers are from SAIF-annotated post-simulation switching activity analysis in Vivado, providing realistic toggle-rate data from actual benchmark execution. The observed near-parity across depths (0.298--0.300\,W) is consistent across the three variants measured (5-, 6-, 7-stage). Power data for the 4-stage and 8-stage variants was not collected; extending the SAIF analysis to all five depths would strengthen the power comparison.

\textbf{SGF predictor scope.} SGF targets GHR-based predictors (gshare and its derivatives). Extension to tournament or TAGE predictors, which maintain multiple history lengths, would require per-table speculative histories and is left to future work. The crc32 marginal disbenefit (+167 mispredictions) demonstrates that SGF is not universally positive, though the effect is negligible in absolute terms.

\section{Conclusion}
% ====================================================================

This paper presents Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a lightweight microarchitectural mechanism that eliminates the stale-GHR branch-prediction degradation (Mechanism~B) in deep FPGA pipelines. SGF reduces mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark and 22.7\% on statemate in 7-stage pipelines, at a cost of 0.5--0.7\% area with no $F_{\max}$ degradation. The mechanism pushes deep-pipeline misprediction counts below the shallow-pipeline baseline because the speculative GHR reflects a more accurate approximation of the true branch history than any committed GHR operating with nonzero update latency.

The SGF result rests on a controlled measurement infrastructure: five RV32IM pipeline variants (4 through 8 stages) sharing identical RTL on Artix-7, 50 post-place-and-route synthesis runs, seven benchmark workloads producing 35 validated CPI data points, and the separation of pipeline-depth CPI overhead into Mechanism~A (penalty scaling, inherent) and Mechanism~B (stale GHR, correctable). A first-principles analytical model achieves $R^2 = 0.977$ across all 35 data points.

The broader findings reinforce the FPGA-specific nature of the pipeline-depth tradeoff. Artix-7 exhibits a two-tier frequency structure: the execute-stage split at depth 6 produces a 56\% frequency jump ($p < 10^{-48}$, Cohen's $d = 18.5$), while further pipeline stages yield no additional frequency benefit. The 6-stage design achieves the highest baseline throughput (64.1\,MIPS on CoreMark). The classic 5-stage RISC pipeline is a dominated design point on this fabric. Cross-fabric validation on Kintex UltraScale confirms that both the frequency structure and the Mechanism~A/B separation persist across Xilinx families.

For practitioners: any FPGA pipeline with 3 or more stages of predictor update latency should implement SGF or an equivalent speculative-update mechanism. The 0.5--0.7\% area cost is negligible; the 17--31\% misprediction reduction is not. Future work includes extending SGF to tournament and TAGE predictor architectures and validating on Intel FPGA fabrics.

The complete RTL, testbenches, synthesis scripts, and benchmark programs are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/devanshjoshi08/RISCV-RV32IM-Processor}.
