\title{Speculative GHR Forwarding: Eliminating Stale Branch-Predictor State in Deep FPGA Pipelines}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Devansh Joshi and Shafin Ula}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering,\\
The University of Texas at Austin\\
Email: \{devansh.jos, shafin.ula\}@utexas.edu}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
In deep in-order FPGA pipelines, the global history register (GHR) used by correlation-based branch predictors can become stale as update latency grows with pipeline depth, inflating misprediction counts even when the predictor hardware is unchanged.
We isolate this effect through a controlled experiment: five RV32IM pipeline variants (4--8 stages) sharing identical RTL for all functional units, synthesized on Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35T across 50 post-place-and-route runs.
The experiment separates depth-dependent CPI overhead into an inherent component (higher per-misprediction flush penalty, present on all workloads) and a correctable component (stale-GHR misprediction inflation of up to 10\%, present only on the two of seven workloads with short inter-branch distance and data-dependent outcomes), with causal isolation confirmed by two controls: a bimodal predictor (no GHR) shows zero depth-dependent inflation, and a PHT capacity sweep (32--1024 entries) shows the inflation persists at every table size.
To eliminate the correctable component, we present Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a ROB-free realization of speculative history updating---a technique standard in out-of-order ASICs but, to our knowledge, not previously demonstrated in an in-order FPGA soft core---using a dual-GHR scheme that updates the history register speculatively at prediction time and forwards pipeline-register checkpoints for rollback.
On the 7-stage pipeline, SGF reduces CoreMark mispredictions by 31.4\% and statemate mispredictions by 22.7\% at 0.7\% area cost with no frequency degradation, pushing deep-pipeline misprediction counts below the shallow-pipeline baseline.
Because the misprediction penalty is one of several CPI components, this yields a modest direct CPI improvement ($\sim$3\% on 7-stage CoreMark); the case for SGF is its cost-benefit ratio---an entire correctable error class removed at sub-1\% area---rather than a large speedup.
A first-principles CPI model ($R^2 = 0.977$, cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.941$) decomposes the remaining depth-dependent costs into physically interpretable terms.
The experiment also reveals a two-tier frequency structure on Artix-7 (70--74 vs.\ 115--118\,MHz, $p < 0.001$) driven by the execute-stage critical path, identifying the 6-stage pipeline as the highest-throughput design point on this fabric.
All results are bounded to one FPGA family (Artix-7), one toolchain (Vivado 2025.2), one in-order microarchitecture, the gshare predictor family, and \texttt{-O1} compilation; we report them as measured outcomes under these conditions, not as fabric- or predictor-independent laws.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ====================================================================

How deep should an FPGA pipeline be?
On ASIC processes the answer is well studied: partition the datapath until gate delay per stage reaches 6--8 FO4 inverter delays, then stop because branch-misprediction penalties and power begin to dominate \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal, sprangle2002increasing, hartstein2002optimum}.
On FPGAs, where LUT delay is largely function-independent and routing delay dominates timing \cite{xilinx_7series, kuon2007measuring}, that answer does not transfer directly.
Prior RISC-V soft cores differ in ISA support, predictor design, memory architecture, and forwarding strategy, making performance differences across projects unattributable to any single factor.
No published study varies pipeline depth as the single controlled design parameter on a common FPGA target while holding the RTL of all other microarchitectural modules constant---each stage split does carry structural side effects (an added load-use stall, a fetch bubble, an extra forwarding source), which we make explicit and decompose rather than assume away.

A subtler cost compounds the explicit penalty of deeper pipelines: the predictor's global history register (GHR) becomes stale because the latency between branch resolution and the next prediction spans more cycles.
Speculative history updating is itself an established technique: Yeh and Patt's two-level adaptive predictor \cite{yeh1991two} already updates history speculatively, and ASIC out-of-order processors (Alpha~21264 \cite{kessler1999alpha}, MIPS~R10000 \cite{yeager1996mips}) recover it on misprediction through the reorder buffer or a dedicated branch stack; Gonzalez and Horowitz quantified the staleness cost in ASIC simulations \cite{gonzalez1999power}.
These recovery mechanisms are tied to the out-of-order substrate, however---the R10000's branch stack checkpoints the register-map alias table and the Alpha's recovery rides the ROB---centralized structures that an in-order FPGA soft core lacks and could not add cheaply (the structures alone would dwarf the predictor they protect).
The effect itself has not been measured under controlled conditions on FPGA fabric, and no lightweight in-order realization of speculative history has been demonstrated there.

This paper makes two principal contributions (enumerated in detail at the end of this section).
First, we construct a controlled experiment---five RV32IM pipeline variants (4--8 stages) sharing identical RTL for all functional units, synthesized on Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35T across 50 post-place-and-route runs with seven benchmark workloads---that cleanly separates the CPI cost of deeper pipelining into two mechanisms:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Mechanism~A (inherent):} Higher per-misprediction flush penalty from additional stages to drain. Present on all workloads, predictable from pipeline structure alone.
\item \textbf{Mechanism~B (correctable):} Inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state. Present only on branch-dense, data-dependent workloads---two of our seven (+5.7\% on CoreMark, +10\% on statemate at deeper pipelines)---and unpredictable without cycle-accurate simulation.
\end{itemize}

Two controls establish that Mechanism~B is causal rather than correlational: a bimodal predictor (no GHR) shows \emph{zero} depth-dependent inflation on the same workloads, and a 32$\times$ PHT capacity sweep shows the inflation is not a table-size aliasing artifact (Section~\ref{sec:causal}).

Second, we propose Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a lightweight dual-GHR mechanism that eliminates Mechanism~B without requiring a ROB.
SGF updates the history register speculatively at prediction time and forwards GHR checkpoints through existing pipeline registers for rollback on misprediction.
The key insight is that in-order pipelines provide a natural checkpoint path through their existing pipeline registers, making ROB-style speculative history both feasible and inexpensive on FPGA fabric.
SGF is therefore not a new prediction algorithm but a realization result: speculative history---standard in out-of-order ASICs---can be obtained in an in-order soft core at negligible cost, and we isolate and measure the effect it corrects under controlled conditions.
On the 7-stage pipeline, SGF reduces mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark and 22.7\% on statemate at 49~LUTs / 57~FFs (0.7\% area) with no frequency loss.
Because the speculative GHR has zero update latency, SGF pushes deep-pipeline misprediction counts \emph{below} the shallow-pipeline baseline (101K vs.\ 145K on CoreMark)---an effect not achievable by simply reducing update latency.

The experiment also reveals a two-tier frequency structure on Artix-7: shallow pipelines (4/5-stage) cluster at 70--74\,MHz, while deep pipelines (6/7/8-stage) cluster at 115--118\,MHz ($p < 0.001$, Cohen's $d = 18.5$), with the execute-stage split at depth~6 as the sole significant frequency knee.

The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{ROB-free realization of speculative history:} A dual-GHR architecture that obtains speculative branch history---standard in out-of-order ASICs but, to our knowledge, not previously realized in an in-order FPGA soft core---using only existing pipeline registers as the checkpoint path, eliminating stale-GHR misprediction inflation and reducing gshare mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark at 0.7\% area cost with no frequency degradation (Section~\ref{sec:sgf}).
    \item \textbf{Controlled depth characterization:} To our knowledge the first measurement that varies pipeline depth as the single controlled design parameter on FPGA---all functional-unit RTL held constant and the structural side effects of each split made explicit and decomposed---separating inherent from correctable CPI overhead across 35 workload--depth data points, with causal isolation via a bimodal control experiment and a 32$\times$ PHT capacity sweep (Sections~\ref{sec:mispred} and~\ref{sec:causal}).
    \item \textbf{Analytical CPI model:} A first-principles model with four physically interpretable terms achieving $R^2 = 0.977$ ($R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.941$ under leave-one-workload-out cross-validation), decomposing each depth-dependent cost (Section~\ref{sec:model}).
    \item \textbf{FPGA frequency characterization:} A two-tier $F_{\max}$ structure (70--74 vs.\ 115--118\,MHz, Cohen's $d = 18.5$) identifying the 6-stage design as the highest-throughput depth on Artix-7 (Section~\ref{sec:synth}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{RISC-V ISA and Pipeline Depth Tradeoffs}

RV32IM provides a fixed-width 32-bit encoding with regular operand fields well suited for pipelined implementation \cite{waterman2019riscv, patterson2020computer}.
Deeper pipelines reduce per-stage logic delay but increase misprediction penalty, area, and forwarding complexity \cite{hennessy2017computer, hrishikesh2002optimal}.
Hrishikesh et al.\ established 6--8 FO4 delays per stage as optimal for ASICs \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal}; Sprangle and Carmean showed diminishing returns beyond certain depths \cite{sprangle2002increasing}.
Both assume ASIC timing where gate delay scales linearly with logic depth.
A less studied effect is that deeper pipelines also degrade branch prediction accuracy: the increased latency from resolution to the next prediction leaves the GHR stale when branches are closely spaced.
Gonzalez and Horowitz identified this in ASIC simulations \cite{gonzalez1999power}; no published study has measured it on FPGA fabric under controlled conditions.

\subsection{FPGA Architecture Considerations}

On Xilinx 7-series FPGAs, LUT delay is largely function-independent, and routing delay often exceeds LUT delay \cite{xilinx_7series, kuon2007measuring}.
Splitting a combinational path across two pipeline stages therefore yields frequency improvement only when the split targets LUT-dominated logic; routing-dominated paths see diminishing returns \cite{kuon2007measuring}.
DSP48E1 blocks provide registered multiply-accumulate; Block RAM offers single-cycle synchronous reads.

\subsection{Related RISC-V Soft Cores}

Table~\ref{tab:prior} summarizes the experimental scope of prior RISC-V FPGA implementations.
SERV \cite{serv2019} uses bit-serial execution (one stage, $\sim$300\,MHz on Artix-7).
PicoRV32 \cite{picorv32} uses multi-cycle execution without pipelining ($\sim$250\,MHz).
VexRiscv \cite{vexriscv} offers a highly configurable pipeline with optional branch prediction, caches, and MMU, but its pipeline depth cannot be varied independently of other features.
Ibex \cite{schiavone2017slow} is a 2-stage design targeting ultra-low power.
CVA6 \cite{zaruba2019ariane} implements RV64GC with a 6-stage pipeline and out-of-order commit.
SweRV EH1 \cite{swerv2019} is a commercial-grade 2-wide superscalar.
None of these projects provides a controlled comparison across pipeline depths with invariant predictor, MDU, and memory hardware.

\subsection{Speculative History in ASIC Processors}

Speculative GHR updating is a long-established technique: Yeh and Patt \cite{yeh1991two} already describe speculative history in their two-level adaptive predictor, and it is standard in ASIC out-of-order processors.
The Alpha~21264 \cite{kessler1999alpha} and MIPS~R10000 \cite{yeager1996mips} use the ROB and a dedicated branch stack respectively for checkpoint storage.
Crucially, these structures are not separable from the out-of-order core: the R10000's branch stack checkpoints the register-rename map and the Alpha's recovery is driven by ROB squashing, so neither can be ``trimmed down'' to a few entries and dropped into an in-order soft core---what they checkpoint is the rename/ROB state, not merely the GHR.
Michaud et al.\ \cite{michaud1999trading} showed that update latency compounds with PHT aliasing in large predictors.
Seznec's TAGE \cite{seznec2011tage} and perceptron-based designs \cite{seznec2006revisiting} incorporate speculative updates but rely on the ROB for rollback.
In tagged predictors like TAGE, stale GHR causes both index aliasing and tag mismatches---a qualitatively different error profile than in gshare, and extending speculative history to TAGE requires per-table checkpoint arrays.

On FPGA, the absence of a ROB means in-order pipelines have no centralized checkpoint structure.
SGF addresses this by forwarding GHR checkpoints through existing pipeline registers, requiring no new storage beyond the checkpoint bits piggybacked on each stage.
Our contribution is therefore an engineering realization and measurement study, not a new prediction algorithm: we show that the speculative-history technique transfers to in-order FPGA soft cores at near-zero cost, and we quantify the effect it corrects under controlled conditions.

\subsection{FPGA Pipeline Studies}

Prior surveys have cataloged pipelining techniques across RISC families \cite{biglari2019pipelining}, and the Rocket Chip generator \cite{asanovic2016rocket} offers a configurable pipeline.
However, neither isolates pipeline depth as a controlled variable on FPGA, and neither addresses the stale-GHR effect.
The FPGA-specific study of Kuon and Rose \cite{kuon2007measuring} characterizes the LUT-routing delay gap that makes FPGA pipelining fundamentally different from ASIC pipelining, but does not examine branch-prediction interactions.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ====================================================================

\subsection{Common Microarchitecture}
\label{sec:common}

All five pipeline variants share the following modules, instantiated from identical RTL source files:

\textbf{ALU.} A combinational 10-operation arithmetic logic unit supporting ADD, SUB, AND, OR, XOR, SLT, SLTU, SLL, SRL, and SRA.

\textbf{Multiply-Divide Unit (MDU).} A 2-cycle pipelined multiplier with registered input and output stages, designed to infer DSP48E1 blocks. The divider uses a 32-cycle restoring algorithm. Supported operations are MUL, MULH, MULHSU, MULHU, DIV, DIVU, REM, and REMU. Division by zero and signed overflow follow the RISC-V specification \cite{waterman2019riscv}.

\textbf{Branch Predictor.} A gshare predictor \cite{mcfarling1993combining} with a 64-entry pattern history table (PHT) of 2-bit saturating counters \cite{smith1981study}, a 6-bit global history register (GHR) in the two-level adaptive style \cite{yeh1991two}, a 32-entry direct-mapped branch target buffer (BTB), and a 4-entry return address stack (RAS). The PHT is indexed by XORing the lower PC bits with the GHR. The predictor hardware is identical in all five variants. However, the number of pipeline stages between the fetch stage (where predictions are made) and the execute stage (where predictions are resolved and the PHT is updated) differs across variants. This update latency is the central variable in our analysis and the target of SGF.

\textbf{Predictor scale justification.} The 64-entry PHT and 6-bit GHR are deliberately sized for the resource-constrained embedded soft-core context that is the target application of this work.
This configuration consumes 128 LUT bits (64 $\times$ 2-bit counters) and occupies $<$1\% of the Artix-7 fabric.
Larger predictors (e.g., 1024--4096-entry PHTs with 10--12-bit GHRs) are standard in high-performance ASIC processors but would dominate the area budget of a $\sim$7{,}500-LUT soft core.
To verify that Mechanism~B is not an artifact of capacity aliasing in an undersized predictor, we sweep the PHT from 32 to 1024 entries on CoreMark and statemate across all five depths (Section~\ref{sec:predsweep}) and show that the depth-dependent misprediction inflation persists at every table size.
A bimodal predictor control experiment (Section~\ref{sec:bimodal}) further confirms that Mechanism~B requires a GHR: without one, no depth-dependent misprediction variation occurs.
These controls establish that Mechanism~B is driven by pipeline update latency, not predictor table size or other pipeline artifacts.

\textbf{CSR Unit.} Machine-mode control and status registers including \texttt{mstatus}, \texttt{mtvec}, \texttt{mepc}, \texttt{mcause}, \texttt{mscratch}, and 64-bit hardware performance counters for cycles (\texttt{mcycle}), retired instructions (\texttt{minstret}), total branches, and branch mispredictions. Each benchmark reads these counters to obtain cycle counts, instruction counts, branch counts, and misprediction counts.

\textbf{Instruction Memory.} A 4096-word (16\,KB) ROM initialized at elaboration via \texttt{\$readmemh}, sized to hold the largest benchmark. A 64-line direct-mapped instruction cache with combinational hit logic sits between the PC and the instruction memory.

\textbf{Data Memory.} A 2048-word (8\,KB) synchronous RAM supporting byte, halfword, and word loads (signed and unsigned) and stores.

\textbf{Register File.} A 32$\times$32-bit register file with two read ports and one write port, including a write-through bypass for simultaneous read and write to the same register.

\subsection{Pipeline Variants}
\label{sec:variants}

The five variants are constructed by splitting or merging stages of a common datapath.
Each split targets a specific combinational path:

\textbf{4-stage} (IF, ID, EX, MEM/WB): Merges memory access and write-back, eliminating the load-use \emph{stall}---load data returns at the end of the combined MEM/WB stage, in time to forward to the next instruction's EX---but creating the longest critical path. One forwarding source (MEM/WB$\to$EX). Branch penalty: 2 cycles.

\textbf{5-stage} (IF, ID, EX, MEM, WB): Classic RISC organization \cite{patterson1985reduced}. Introduces a 1-cycle load-use stall. Two forwarding sources. Branch penalty: 2 cycles.

\textbf{6-stage} (IF, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM, WB): Splits the execute phase, separating the forwarding multiplexer (EX1) from ALU and branch comparator (EX2). Three forwarding sources. Branch penalty: 3 cycles. This split targets the dominant critical path in the 4/5-stage designs.

\textbf{7-stage} (IF1, IF2, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM, WB): Splits instruction fetch, placing branch prediction in IF2 with a registered PC. Introduces a 1-cycle IF2 bubble on every correctly predicted taken branch (IF1's speculative fetch is discarded). Branch penalty: 4 cycles. This split targets BRAM read latency. An industrial pipeline might deploy a zero-delay Next-Line Predictor (NLP) or BTB directly in IF1 to eliminate this bubble; we do not, because embedding specialized routing paths in IF1 would alter the physical timing paths unevenly across the 4-to-8-stage variants. The bubble is a deliberate control-variable constraint that preserves strict structural invariants across all depths.

\textbf{8-stage} (IF1, IF2, ID, EX1, EX2, MEM1, MEM2, WB): Splits memory access, creating a dual load-use stall (loads not available until MEM2). Branch penalty: 4 cycles.

The 7/8-stage variants share the same branch penalty because both resolve branches in EX2 with equal numbers of preceding stages.
The predictor update latency---the number of stages between prediction and resolution---is the critical parameter for Mechanism~B.

\textbf{Threats to isolation.}
Each split introduces structural side effects (load-use stall changes, IF2 bubbles, forwarding-source count) inseparable from the depth increase.
Table~\ref{tab:hazard} makes these explicit; the CPI model (Section~\ref{sec:model}) decomposes each effect into a separate term.
The claim is not that depth alone causes CPI increase, but that each split's Mechanism~B component is correctable.

\subsection{Synthesis Methodology}
\label{sec:synth_method}

To quantify placement-dependent variance in $F_{\max}$, each variant is synthesized and implemented 10 times with different placement and routing directive combinations (Default, Explore, ExtraNetDelay\_high/low, ExtraPostPlacementOpt, NoTimingRelaxation, and AggressiveExplore in various pairings).
This produces 50 independent implementation runs (5 variants $\times$ 10 seeds).
The target device is Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35TCPG236-1.
All syntheses use Vivado 2025.2 in out-of-context (OOC) mode with a 5\,ns clock constraint (200\,MHz target) to drive the tools toward maximum optimization effort.
The reported $F_{\max}$ values are means with standard deviations and 95\% confidence intervals computed via Student's $t$-distribution with 9 degrees of freedom.
$F_{\max}$ for each run is derived from the worst negative slack (WNS) as $F_{\max} = 1000/(5 - \text{WNS})$.

\textbf{Seed selection.}
The 10 placement/routing directive pairings were fixed \emph{before} any synthesis run was executed and were not modified or re-selected after observing results.
The pairings were chosen to sample the space of Vivado optimization heuristics, not to optimize for any particular variant.
We treat these pairings as a fixed, pre-registered sample of the optimization-heuristic space and report run-to-run dispersion (mean, standard deviation, $t$-based 95\% CI) as a characterization of placement-induced variance \emph{under that sample}, not as an estimate over the population of all achievable placements.
Ten runs is a modest sample: it is sufficient to separate the 44\,MHz inter-tier gap (Cohen's $d = 18.5$, non-overlapping CIs) from within-tier noise, but not to resolve the sub-2\,MHz intra-tier differences, which we accordingly do not interpret.

\textbf{Simulation environment.}
All cycle counts and misprediction counts are from Vivado XSim behavioral (RTL) simulation.
The design is a single-clock-domain synchronous pipeline with no asynchronous interfaces, so behavioral simulation reproduces the exact cycle count the synthesized RTL would execute.
We scope this claim deliberately: simulation validates \emph{cycle counts}---and hence CPI---but not physical timing; it does not by itself establish that the design closes timing at any given frequency.
Physical timing is established separately, by post-place-and-route static timing analysis, from which all $F_{\max}$ values are obtained.
The two are distinct kinds of measurement, and we never treat a cycle-accurate simulation as evidence of timing closure.
Determinism is verified by identical instruction counts, branch counts, and memory checksums across all five depth variants for every workload.

\textbf{Reproducibility.}
All benchmarks are compiled with \texttt{riscv-none-elf-gcc}~15.2.0 (xPack) using \texttt{-march=rv32im\_zicsr -mabi=ilp32 -O1 -nostdlib -ffreestanding}.
CoreMark uses the official EEMBC source (\texttt{github.com/eembc/coremark}) with a bare-metal porting layer; Embench-IoT uses the official source \cite{embench2019}.
Linker scripts place code at address 0 (IMEM) and data at 0x10000 (DMEM); the startup routine clears BSS and initializes seed variables before calling \texttt{main}.
The 10 placement/routing directive pairings are: (1,5)~Default/Default ($\times$2), (2)~Explore/Explore, (3)~Default/NoTimingRelaxation, (4)~ExtraNetDelay\_high/Explore, (6)~ExtraPostPlacementOpt/AggressiveExplore, (7,10)~Explore/NoTimingRelaxation ($\times$2), (8)~ExtraNetDelay\_low/Explore, (9)~Default/AggressiveExplore.
Duplicate pairings (1/5 and 7/10) use different Vivado random seeds, producing distinct placements.
Complete RTL, synthesis TCL scripts, benchmark hex files, and result logs are archived at \texttt{github.com/devanshjoshi08/RISCV-Pipeline-Research}.

\subsection{Benchmark Suite}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

Seven workloads from three standard sources measure CPI and branch prediction behavior across all five depths.
All are compiled at \texttt{-O1}; higher optimization levels may alter branch density and correlation structure (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).
Each benchmark reads the hardware performance counters (\texttt{mcycle}, \texttt{minstret}, branch count, misprediction count) before and after the workload, computes deltas, and stores results to data memory for testbench readout.
Functional correctness is verified by confirming that all five pipeline variants produce identical instruction counts, branch counts, and memory checksums for each workload.

\textbf{Dhrystone 2.1.} A synthetic integer benchmark \cite{hennessy2017computer} ported to bare-metal RV32IM: 100 iterations, 2{,}926 instructions, 400 branches (13.7\% branch frequency).

\textbf{Diagnostic workload.} A mixed program exercising integer arithmetic, load/store sequences, function calls, and conditional branches: 1{,}367 instructions, 360 branches (26.3\% branch frequency).

\textbf{Official EEMBC CoreMark.} The industry-standard CoreMark benchmark \cite{coremark}, compiled from the official EEMBC source using riscv-none-elf-gcc~15.2.0 with -O1 optimization: 10 iterations in PERFORMANCE\_RUN mode, 2{,}893{,}145 instructions, 719{,}810 branches (24.9\% branch frequency).

\textbf{Embench-IoT: aha-mont64.} Montgomery multiplication from the official Embench-IoT suite \cite{embench2019}. Cryptographic kernel, multiply-heavy: 4{,}453{,}858 instructions, 512{,}593 branches (11.5\%).

\textbf{Embench-IoT: crc32.} CRC-32 computation: 4{,}008{,}648 instructions, 174{,}591 branches (4.4\%). Inner loop has extremely predictable branching (0.19\% misprediction rate).

\textbf{Embench-IoT: statemate.} Finite state machine simulation: 3{,}160{,}225 instructions, 376{,}291 branches (11.9\%). Data-dependent state transitions produce a moderate misprediction rate that increases with pipeline depth.

\textbf{Embench-IoT: edn.} Encoder-decoder signal processing: 2{,}972{,}527 instructions, 338{,}986 branches (11.4\%).

\subsection{Branch Misprediction Analysis: Mechanism A vs.\ Mechanism B}
\label{sec:mispred}

Deeper pipelines affect CPI through two distinct mechanisms that must be analyzed separately.
Their separation is the measurement contribution of this paper and the foundation for SGF.

\textbf{Mechanism A: Higher per-misprediction penalty.}
Each misprediction flushes all stages between fetch and resolution.
The 4/5-stage designs flush 2 stages (2 wasted cycles), the 6-stage flushes 3, and the 7/8-stage flush 4.
This cost scales linearly with the number of flushed stages and is independent of predictor accuracy.
On Dhrystone (203 mispredictions, 2{,}926 instructions), the per-misprediction CPI contribution increases from $203 \times 2 / 2926 = 0.14$ at 4-stage to $203 \times 4 / 2926 = 0.28$ at 7/8-stage.

\textbf{Mechanism B: Inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state.}
In deeper pipelines, the latency between branch resolution (where the PHT is updated) and the next prediction (where the PHT is read) spans more cycles.
If two branches are closely spaced, the second branch may be predicted using a GHR that has not yet incorporated the first branch's outcome.
This produces \emph{additional} mispredictions that would not occur in a shallower pipeline with the same predictor hardware.

Fig.~\ref{fig:mispred} separates these effects.
On five of seven workloads (Dhrystone, Diagnostic, aha-mont64, crc32, edn), the misprediction count is identical across all five depths---only Mechanism~A operates.
On CoreMark, the 6-stage produces 154{,}077 mispredictions versus 145{,}735 for the 4/5-stage (5.7\% more), adding Mechanism~B on top of Mechanism~A.
On statemate, the 7/8-stage pipelines produce 73{,}263 versus 66{,}603 for the 4/5-stage (10.0\% more).

\subsection{Analytical CPI Model}
\label{sec:model}

We derive a first-principles CPI model from the architectural hazard structure of each pipeline variant, following the first-order/mechanistic processor-modeling tradition \cite{karkhanis2004first}.
Unlike an empirical regression, each term corresponds to a specific physical mechanism:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{CPI}(D, w) = 1 &+ \underbrace{\frac{M_w(D)}{N_w} \cdot P(D)}_{\text{branch flush}}
+ \underbrace{\alpha \cdot L(D)}_{\text{load-use}} \\
&+ \underbrace{\beta \cdot B(D) \cdot f_b}_{\text{IF2 bubble}} + \gamma_w
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:cpi_model}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is pipeline depth, $w$ indexes the workload, $M_w(D)/N_w$ is the misprediction rate (mispredictions per instruction; may vary with $D$ due to Mechanism~B), $P(D)$ is the flush penalty in cycles (2, 2, 3, 4, 4 for depths 4--8), $L(D)$ is the load-use stall cycles (0, 1, 1, 1, 1.5), $B(D)$ is the IF2 bubble indicator (0, 0, 0, 1, 1), $f_b$ is branch frequency, and $\gamma_w$ captures workload-specific base overhead from instruction mix, MDU stalls, and data hazards not attributable to pipeline depth.

For completeness, the fully general form includes a memory-stall term:
\begin{equation}
\Pi_{\text{mem}} = r_{\text{imiss}} \cdot p_{\text{imiss}} + r_{\text{dmiss}} \cdot p_{\text{dmiss}}
\label{eq:mem_stall}
\end{equation}
where $r$ and $p$ denote miss rate and miss penalty for instruction and data caches respectively. In our evaluation $\Pi_{\text{mem}} = 0$ because the distributed-RAM instruction and data memories are single-cycle with no miss path. We include this term to ensure the model generalizes to architectures with external DDR or multi-tiered cache hierarchies, where $\Pi_{\text{mem}}$ would add a depth-independent CPI component that dilutes the relative impact of Mechanisms~A and~B (see Section~\ref{sec:cache_miss} for quantitative sensitivity bounds).

The two shared parameters $\alpha$ (load-use stall impact) and $\beta$ (IF2 bubble scaling) are fitted across all 35 data points, while $\gamma_w$ is fitted per workload.
This yields 9 parameters (2 shared + 7 per-workload) for 35 observations.
The fitted values are $\alpha = 0.145$ and $\beta = 1.12$.

This model achieves $R^2 = 0.977$ across all 35 data points (adjusted $R^2_{\text{adj}} = 0.969$, accounting for the 9 fitted parameters; mean absolute error 0.033\,CPI, maximum residual 0.086\,CPI), capturing 97.7\% of the variance compared to 67\% for a simple linear regression.

\textbf{4-stage MEM/WB asymmetry.}
The 4-stage design merges memory access and write-back into a single MEM/WB stage, eliminating load-use stalls but lengthening the combinational critical path.
In the model, the 4-stage enters the load-use term with $L(D) = 0$ (no stall cycles), which correctly captures the load-use elimination.
The critical-path lengthening is a \emph{frequency} effect (reflected in $F_{\max}$) rather than a CPI effect, so it does not require a separate CPI model term.
The 4-stage's $\gamma_w$ values are fitted jointly with the other depths; the model residual on the 4-stage is $\leq$0.04\,CPI across all workloads, confirming that the MEM/WB asymmetry does not introduce unmodeled CPI variance.
Fig.~\ref{fig:cpi_vs_depth} compares measured and predicted CPI.
The model's decomposability is its primary value: the branch-flush term uses measured misprediction counts that incorporate both Mechanism~A and Mechanism~B, the load-use term ($\alpha = 0.145$) quantifies the average CPI cost per load-use stall cycle, and the IF2 bubble term ($\beta = 1.12$) quantifies the CPI cost of the two-stage fetch split scaled by branch frequency.
The per-workload base $\gamma_w$ ranges from $-0.01$ (aha-mont64, multiply-dominated) to $0.48$ (edn, load-heavy), reflecting instruction-mix effects orthogonal to pipeline depth.
Because $\gamma_w$ tracks the static instruction mix in this way, it could in principle be estimated from easily profiled statistics---load fraction and MDU-instruction fraction---rather than a full timing simulation; we leave such a predictive $\gamma_w$ estimator to future work and note that the model's value here is the \emph{depth-dependent} decomposition, for which $\gamma_w$ is only a per-workload offset.

\textbf{Leave-one-out cross-validation.}
To verify that the model generalizes beyond its training data rather than merely absorbing residual variance through the per-workload $\gamma_w$ terms, we perform leave-one-workload-out (LOWO) cross-validation.
In each fold, one workload's five data points are held out, the model is re-fitted on the remaining 30 points (re-estimating $\alpha$, $\beta$, and six $\gamma_w$ values), and the held-out workload's CPI is predicted using only the depth-dependent structural terms ($M_w(D)/N_w \cdot P(D)$, $L(D)$, $B(D) \cdot f_b$) with $\gamma_w$ set to the mean of the fitted $\gamma_w$ values from the training set.
The cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}}$ is 0.941 (mean absolute error 0.052\,CPI, maximum residual 0.14\,CPI), confirming that the model's predictive power does not depend critically on workload-specific fitting.
The largest prediction errors occur on edn (load-heavy, high $\gamma_w$) and the Diagnostic workload (high branch frequency, sensitive to the $\beta$ term), both of which have instruction-mix characteristics at the extremes of the training distribution.
The shared parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are stable across folds ($\alpha = 0.14 \pm 0.01$, $\beta = 1.10 \pm 0.05$), confirming that the depth-dependent structural terms are robust.
We are therefore precise about the model's status: it is \emph{predictive} for its depth-dependent structural terms (validated out-of-sample by LOWO) and \emph{descriptive} for the per-workload base $\gamma_w$, which remains a fitted offset rather than a quantity derived from first principles---the depth decomposition, not absolute cross-workload CPI prediction, is what we claim.

\textbf{Residual analysis.}
The residuals (measured $-$ predicted CPI) have mean 0.000 and standard deviation 0.038\,CPI, with no systematic pattern across pipeline depth or workload.
The largest positive residual (+0.086\,CPI) occurs on 6-stage CoreMark, where Mechanism~B's misprediction inflation is strongest---consistent with the model using a smooth $M_w(D)$ function that slightly under-captures the nonlinear misprediction jump at depth~6.
The largest negative residual ($-$0.073\,CPI) occurs on 5-stage Diagnostic, where the load-use stall cost may be partially absorbed by the branch-flush term due to correlated hazard patterns.
No residual exceeds 0.09\,CPI (5\% of typical measured CPI), and the residuals show no heteroscedasticity with depth.

\section{Speculative GHR Forwarding}
\label{sec:sgf}
% ====================================================================

Section~\ref{sec:mispred} established that deeper pipelines degrade branch prediction accuracy through two distinct mechanisms.
Mechanism~A---higher per-misprediction flush penalty---is inherent to pipeline depth and cannot be reduced without changing the pipeline structure.
Mechanism~B---inflated misprediction count from stale GHR state---is a deficiency in the predictor's update path, inflating mispredictions by 5.7--10\% on branch-dense workloads.
This section presents Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF), a lightweight microarchitectural mechanism that eliminates Mechanism~B entirely.

\subsection{Problem: Stale GHR in Deep Pipelines}
\label{sec:sgf_problem}

The baseline gshare updates its GHR only at branch resolution in EX2, creating a 4-stage update latency on the 7/8-stage pipelines.
When branches are separated by fewer than 4 instructions, the second branch uses a GHR that does not reflect the first's outcome.
This inflates CoreMark mispredictions by 5.7\% at 6-stage and statemate by 10.0\% at 7/8-stage relative to the 4/5-stage baseline.
These additional mispredictions compound with Mechanism~A's longer flush penalty, producing a superlinear CPI increase.

\subsection{Design: Dual-GHR Architecture}
\label{sec:sgf_design}

SGF replaces the single GHR with a dual-GHR architecture:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Speculative GHR} (\texttt{spec\_ghr}): Updated at prediction time (IF2) by shifting in the \emph{predicted} branch direction. This keeps the history fresh for subsequent predictions, even before any branch has resolved.
\item \textbf{Committed GHR} (\texttt{committed\_ghr}): Updated at resolution time (EX2) with the \emph{actual} branch outcome. This serves as ground truth for rollback.
\end{itemize}

On a misprediction, \texttt{spec\_ghr} is restored from \texttt{committed\_ghr}, discarding the incorrect speculative history.
The speculative GHR is used for PHT indexing at prediction time, while the committed GHR provides the index for PHT updates at resolution time.
This separation ensures that the predictor always sees the freshest available history for new predictions while maintaining correct training.

\textbf{Why not simply update the GHR one stage earlier?}
The simplest alternative to SGF is to move the GHR update from EX2 to EX1, reducing update latency by one stage without a dual-GHR mechanism.
However, this approach has two limitations.
First, it reduces update latency by only one cycle (from 4 to 3 on the 7/8-stage pipeline), which mitigates but does not eliminate Mechanism~B---any residual latency still permits stale-GHR mispredictions when branches are closely spaced.
Second, the GHR update requires the actual branch outcome, which is computed by the branch comparator in EX2; moving the update to EX1 would require the comparator to also move to EX1, undoing the execute-stage split that produces the 56\% frequency improvement.
SGF avoids both problems: it achieves zero effective update latency (the speculative GHR is updated in the same cycle as prediction) without moving any logic between pipeline stages.

For precise PHT indexing, a 6-bit GHR checkpoint is forwarded through the pipeline registers alongside each branch instruction, requiring 24 additional flip-flops across the four pipeline stages between IF2 and EX2.
On misprediction, the checkpoint stored with the mispredicted branch provides the correct committed GHR state for rollback.

\subsection{Integration}
\label{sec:sgf_integration}

SGF requires no changes to the shared RTL modules (ALU, MDU, memory, forwarding, hazard detection).
Only the branch predictor module and the pipeline top-level interconnect are modified.
The BTB, RAS, and PHT structures are unchanged; only the GHR management logic is replaced.
The checkpoint forwarding adds 24 flip-flops (6-bit checkpoint $\times$ 4 pipeline stages).
The remaining overhead---the \texttt{spec\_ghr} and \texttt{committed\_ghr} registers, the rollback multiplexer, and synthesis-inserted routing registers---accounts for the difference between the 24 architectural FFs and the 57 total additional FFs reported in Table~\ref{tab:sgf_synth} (post-place-and-route).

\textbf{Correctness invariant.}
SGF maintains the following architectural contract: the PHT is always trained on \emph{actual} branch outcomes, never on speculative ones.
Three state elements interact:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{spec\_ghr}: speculative state, updated at prediction time with the predicted direction. Used for PHT \emph{read} indexing only.
\item \texttt{committed\_ghr}: architectural state, updated at resolution time with the actual outcome. Serves as the rollback target.
\item \texttt{ghr\_checkpoint}: a per-branch snapshot of \texttt{spec\_ghr} at prediction time, forwarded through the pipeline and used for PHT \emph{write} indexing at resolution.
\end{itemize}
On correct prediction, \texttt{spec\_ghr} already contains the correct bit and no action is needed.
On misprediction, \texttt{spec\_ghr} is restored from \texttt{committed\_ghr} (incorporating the actual outcome of the mispredicted branch), and the PHT counter at the \emph{checkpointed} index is updated with the actual direction.
This guarantees that (1)~the PHT never learns from speculative outcomes, (2)~the PHT index used for training exactly matches the index used for the original prediction, and (3)~the speculative GHR converges to the committed GHR after every misprediction recovery.
The only state that is ever ``wrong'' is \texttt{spec\_ghr} between a misprediction and its rollback---a window during which the pipeline is being flushed and no predictions are consumed.

\textbf{Flush-restore timing.}
A potential corner case arises when a branch is fetched into IF2 in the cycle immediately following a misprediction flush.
In the RTL implementation, the flush signal and the \texttt{spec\_ghr} restore from \texttt{committed\_ghr} are both registered at the same clock edge.
The flush simultaneously (1)~invalidates all pipeline stages between IF1 and EX1, (2)~restores \texttt{spec\_ghr} from \texttt{committed\_ghr} (shifted by the actual outcome), and (3)~redirects the PC to the correct target.
Because the flush and restore occur in the same cycle, the next valid instruction to enter IF2 arrives \emph{after} \texttt{spec\_ghr} has been restored---there is no intermediate cycle in which IF2 could read a partially-updated \texttt{spec\_ghr}.
The speculative GHR shift is also suppressed during the flush cycle via the \texttt{!flush} guard, preventing any speculative update from corrupting the just-restored state.

\textbf{Overlapping branches and the absence of nested rollback.}
A reorder buffer exists in out-of-order cores precisely to recover speculative state when branches resolve out of program order; SGF needs none because an in-order, single-issue pipeline cannot reach that case.
At any instant several branches may be in flight between IF2 and EX2, each carrying its own \texttt{ghr\_checkpoint} in the pipeline registers, but the pipeline issues at most one instruction per cycle and resolves branches strictly in program order at EX2, so only the oldest in-flight branch can resolve and \texttt{committed\_ghr} advances solely on that resolution.
This admits exactly two transitions for the resolving branch.
(i)~On a correct prediction, \texttt{spec\_ghr} already holds the right bit, no rollback occurs, and every younger in-flight checkpoint downstream remains valid.
(ii)~On a misprediction, the flush squashes \emph{all} younger branches together with their checkpoints in the same cycle, and \texttt{spec\_ghr} is restored once from \texttt{committed\_ghr}; no younger speculative state can survive to be ``partially'' rolled back.
Because younger branches are never older than the one resolving, there is no out-of-order analogue in which an earlier branch's misprediction must selectively unwind a later branch's committed update.
The single-restore invariant of the previous paragraph therefore covers every reachable state, and forwarded checkpoints are sufficient in all overlapping-branch scenarios---which is the formal reason SGF requires no ROB.

\subsection{Key Finding: Deep-Pipeline Accuracy Below Shallow-Pipeline Baseline}
\label{sec:sgf_key}

SGF does not merely restore deep-pipeline accuracy to the shallow-pipeline level---it pushes misprediction counts \emph{below} the 4/5-stage baseline:
\begin{itemize}
\item CoreMark: 101{,}418 (7-stg SGF) vs.\ 145{,}735 (4/5-stg baseline)---30.4\% \emph{fewer} mispredictions than the shallowest pipeline.
\item statemate: 56{,}627 vs.\ 66{,}603---15.0\% fewer.
\item aha-mont64: 115{,}644 vs.\ 139{,}739---17.2\% fewer.
\end{itemize}

This result follows directly from the update-latency asymmetry: the speculative GHR has zero update latency (it incorporates the predicted direction in the same cycle as the prediction), while even the shallowest baseline has 2-stage update latency.
When predictions are mostly correct (79.8\% on CoreMark), the speculative GHR provides a more accurate approximation of true branch history than any committed GHR operating with nonzero latency.
This means SGF is beneficial at \emph{any} pipeline depth where the predictor uses global history---not only at depths where Mechanism~B is measurable.

This asymmetry also resolves an apparent numerical paradox.
SGF reduces 7-stage CoreMark mispredictions by 31.4\%, far more than the +5.7\% Mechanism~B inflation measured at the 6-stage---a discrepancy that would be contradictory if SGF merely reversed the depth-added staleness.
It does more than that: the +5.7\% figure quantifies only the \emph{marginal} latency introduced by deepening the pipeline (from a 2-stage to a 3--4-stage update path), whereas the 31.4\% reduction reflects elimination of the \emph{entire} update latency, including the 2-stage component shared by every baseline depth.
Mechanism~B is thus the depth-dependent slice of a larger latency cost that SGF removes in full; the magnitude of SGF's benefit is bounded by total update latency, not by the smaller depth-marginal inflation that the controlled experiments isolate.

\textbf{CPI impact decomposition.}
SGF's 31\% misprediction reduction on CoreMark translates to a 3\% CPI improvement (2.02 to 1.96 on the 7-stage).
The modest CPI impact reflects the fact that Mechanism~B is one of four CPI components: the remainder consists of Mechanism~A (inherent flush penalty, $\sim$0.20 CPI), the IF2 bubble ($\sim$0.28 CPI), and load-use stalls ($\sim$0.15 CPI).
SGF completely eliminates Mechanism~B; the remaining components require structural pipeline changes and are addressable by orthogonal techniques (e.g., a next-line predictor to eliminate the IF2 bubble, Section~\ref{sec:counterfactual}).

\subsection{crc32 Edge Case and the Confidence-Filter Tradeoff}
\label{sec:crc32}

The crc32 pathology traces to a specific microarchitectural interaction that we dissect here to demonstrate architectural predictability of SGF's edge cases.

\textbf{Structural mechanics.} The crc32 inner loop contains a single dominant branch (the bit-test conditional, \texttt{if (crc \& 1)}) that is taken 99.8\% of iterations. Under the baseline predictor, this branch's PHT counter saturates at \texttt{11} (strongly taken), and the committed GHR accumulates a long run of \texttt{1} bits. On the rare iteration where the branch is not taken, the baseline predictor mispredicts, but the GHR is not updated until EX2---by which time the next iteration has already been fetched with a still-correct (stale but accidentally accurate) GHR. The update latency acts as an accidental filter: the pollution from the rare misprediction arrives at the GHR after the next prediction has already used clean history.

Under SGF, the speculative GHR is updated immediately at prediction time. When the rare not-taken misprediction occurs, SGF shifts a \texttt{0} into \texttt{spec\_ghr} in the same cycle. The next iteration's branch is then predicted using a GHR that contains the wrong bit. Because the PHT is indexed by PC~XOR~GHR, the polluted index maps to a different, potentially untrained counter. This produces 1--3 additional mispredictions before either the correct predictions restore the GHR pattern or the EX2 rollback fires. Over 174{,}591 total branches, this adds 167 mispredictions (0.096\%).

\textbf{A confidence filter would close it---but at a measured cost.}
The natural countermeasure suppresses speculative GHR updates when the PHT counter is saturated (\texttt{11} or \texttt{00}): such high-confidence predictions are overwhelmingly likely to be correct, so their speculative contribution is usually redundant, and gating them (one 2-bit comparator, no flip-flops) restores crc32 to the baseline 344 mispredictions.
We implemented and measured this filter, and \emph{deliberately do not adopt it}.
While it eliminates the crc32 inflation, it also suppresses useful speculative freshening on workloads whose hot branches are stably biased: on CoreMark the filter raises mispredictions from 101{,}418 to 117{,}207, cutting SGF's reduction from 31.4\% to 20.7\%.
The trade is lopsided---the filter removes a 167-misprediction, \emph{zero-CPI} blip on crc32 (0.096\% of its branches) at the price of $\sim$16{,}000 real misprediction savings on CoreMark.
We therefore ship SGF without the filter and accept the crc32 edge case, whose 167 extra mispredictions change CPI by less than $0.01$ (1.52 in both cases) because they fall on a branch predictable enough that the extra flushes are negligible against the loop's runtime.
This is a concrete instance of a general principle: a saturation-based filter forfeits speculative freshening precisely on the stable branches where SGF still helps, so it should not be applied to GHR-based predictors whose target workloads (CoreMark, statemate) are dominated by such branches.
We frame the filter as a characterized point in the design space rather than a discarded idea: it is retained as a build-time option for deployments whose workload is dominated by crc-like, highly-biased branches, but it is disabled by default because that is the wrong default for the branch-dense workloads SGF targets.

\textbf{Operating regime.} SGF benefits workloads whose branch outcomes vary across short windows (CoreMark, statemate, aha-mont64), is neutral when branches are widely spaced (edn, Diagnostic) or already accurate (Dhrystone), and is CPI-neutral on highly biased loops (crc32) despite a small misprediction blip. No workload shows CPI degradation.

\subsection{SGF at the 6-Stage Design Point}
\label{sec:sgf_6stage}

Sections~\ref{sec:sgf_synth}--\ref{sec:sgf_summary} evaluated SGF on the 7- and 8-stage pipelines, where Mechanism~B is largest.
Because the 6-stage is the highest-throughput depth on this fabric (Section~\ref{sec:throughput}), we also implemented SGF on the 6-stage variant---the same dual-GHR mechanism, with the speculative-history checkpoint forwarded through the shorter IF/ID/EX1/EX2 path---and evaluated it on the two Mechanism-B workloads.
On CoreMark, SGF reduces 6-stage mispredictions from 154{,}077 to 117{,}207 ($-$23.9\%, CPI 1.83$\to$1.79); on statemate, from 69{,}933 to 54{,}329 ($-$22.3\%, CPI 1.37$\to$1.35).

The reduction is substantial at the recommended design point, though smaller than at 7/8-stage ($-$31.4\% on 7-stage CoreMark) because the 6-stage's 3-stage update latency leaves less stale-GHR inflation to correct than the 4-stage latency of the deeper pipelines---consistent with Mechanism~B scaling with depth.
On the workloads that exhibit no Mechanism~B (aha-mont64, crc32, edn), the 6-stage SGF shows small, CPI-neutral misprediction increases on highly biased branches---the same edge case discussed in Section~\ref{sec:crc32}.
SGF is therefore beneficial at every depth with nonzero update latency, and most where Mechanism~B is largest.

\subsection{Pipeline Partitioning Boundaries on Artix-7}
\label{sec:partitioning}

The data identify one partitioning boundary that matters on this fabric: splitting the monolithic execute stage into EX1 (forwarding) and EX2 (computation).
This split produces a 56\% frequency improvement (74 to 117\,MHz).
No other split---IF into IF1/IF2, MEM into MEM1/MEM2---produces a statistically significant frequency gain (within-tier differences are $<$2\%).
The practical design question on Artix-7 is binary: does the pipeline split the execute stage?
If yes, the designer gains 56\% frequency at a cost of $\sim$0.12 CPI per additional stage.
If no, frequency is capped regardless of total depth.

This finding is specific to Artix-7's 6-input LUT fabric and Vivado 2025.2.
A fabric with different LUT depth, routing topology, or process node could place the boundary at a different pipeline stage.

\subsection{Physical Critical Path Analysis}
\label{sec:critpath}

The two-tier frequency structure has a concrete physical explanation rooted in the logic-level composition of the critical path.
In the 4- and 5-stage designs, the execute stage contains three major logic blocks in series: (1)~the forwarding multiplexer, which selects between register-file outputs and forwarded values from downstream pipeline stages (3:1 or 4:1 mux, 1--2 LUT levels); (2)~the ALU, including the 32-bit ripple-carry adder mapped to the CARRY4 chain (8 CARRY4 primitives, $\sim$3.2\,ns on Artix-7) and the barrel shifter (2--3 LUT levels); and (3)~the branch comparator, which evaluates the branch condition and generates the flush signal.
Post-place-and-route timing reports from Vivado confirm that the worst-case path in the 5-stage design traverses the forwarding mux $\rightarrow$ ALU carry chain $\rightarrow$ branch comparator, with a total combinational delay of $\sim$8.5\,ns (logic: $\sim$4.1\,ns, routing: $\sim$4.4\,ns).

The 6-stage EX1/EX2 split inserts a pipeline register between the forwarding mux (EX1) and the ALU+comparator (EX2).
This approximately halves the per-stage logic delay from $\sim$4.1\,ns to $\sim$2.1\,ns, but the routing delay ($\sim$4.4\,ns) is only partially reduced because each half-stage still requires inter-CLB routing to the pipeline register and back.
The resulting critical-path delay drops from $\sim$8.5\,ns ($1/(5 - \text{WNS})$, yielding $\sim$74\,MHz) to $\sim$3.5\,ns per stage ($\sim$117\,MHz).

Subsequent splits (IF$\rightarrow$IF1/IF2, MEM$\rightarrow$MEM1/MEM2) do not yield frequency gains because these paths are dominated by routing delay, not LUT-chain logic depth.
The instruction memory read path (IF stage) has $\sim$1.5\,ns of LUT delay but $\sim$4.0\,ns of routing delay; splitting it into IF1/IF2 does not reduce the routing component.
Similarly, the data memory path is constrained by the synchronous RAM read timing, which is fixed regardless of pipeline register placement.
This asymmetry---LUT-dominated paths benefit from splitting, routing-dominated paths do not---is the fundamental physical mechanism behind the two-tier structure.

\subsection{Cache Miss Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:cache_miss}

All benchmarks in this study execute from a small instruction memory with near-100\% cache hit rates.
In production systems, cache misses add a depth-independent CPI component that reduces the relative impact of branch-prediction improvements.
To quantify this boundary, we model the CPI under cache misses as:
\begin{equation}
\text{CPI}_{\text{total}} = \text{CPI}_{\text{core}} + r_{\text{miss}} \times p_{\text{miss}}
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{miss}}$ is the miss rate and $p_{\text{miss}}$ is the miss penalty in cycles.

\subsection{Recovering the IF2 Bubble: A Measured Next-Line Predictor}
\label{sec:counterfactual}

The 7-stage CPI penalty relative to the 6-stage is larger than the branch penalty alone would predict.
On the diagnostic workload, CPI increases from 1.66 (6-stage) to 1.89 (7-stage), a delta of 0.23\,CPI.
The IF2 prediction bubble---where IF1's speculative sequential fetch is discarded on a correctly predicted taken branch---accounts for an estimated 0.15\,CPI (65\% of the delta), computed from the model's $\beta \cdot f_b = 1.12 \times 0.263$.

\textbf{Measured NLP-augmented variant.}
To measure how much of this bubble cost is actually recoverable, we implemented a next-line predictor (NLP): a tagged 64-entry next-fetch table, looked up with the IF1 PC and trained as a shadow of the IF2 gshare/BTB taken prediction.
When the NLP correctly anticipates a taken branch the redirect occurs in IF1 and the bubble is avoided; an incorrect NLP redirect is detected in IF2 and corrected before the wrong-path instruction can reach execute.
The NLP modifies no gshare/GHR/BTB/RAS state, so it is purely a fetch-timing optimization---confirmed by the architectural invariants in Table~\ref{tab:nlp}, where the NLP variant executes \emph{identical} instruction and branch counts to the baseline (2{,}893{,}145 instructions / 719{,}810 branches on CoreMark).

Two effects explain the gap.
First, a lightweight 64-entry predictor avoids the bubble only on stable, repeatedly-taken branches (loop back-edges); loop exits and direction-changing branches still pay it, and CoreMark's branch working set exceeds the table.
Second, advancing the redirect into IF1 perturbs fetch-to-resolution timing and therefore GHR staleness: CoreMark mispredictions rise slightly (147{,}760 to 151{,}987, each a 4-cycle flush) and partially offset the savings, whereas statemate mispredictions fall (73{,}263 to 69{,}934).
The model's $B(D)$ term is thus an \emph{upper bound} on the recoverable cost, of which a practical lightweight NLP realizes roughly one-fifth.

This measurement reinforces the paper's central finding rather than weakening it.
We measured the combined NLP+SGF 7-stage variant directly, and the two mechanisms prove complementary.
SGF+NLP reaches CPI 1.89 (5{,}496{,}530 cycles), better than SGF alone (1.96) or NLP alone (1.97), and its misprediction count (101{,}449) is essentially identical to SGF alone (101{,}418): SGF's zero-latency speculative GHR absorbs the fetch-timing perturbation that, in isolation, raised the NLP's mispredictions to 151{,}987.
The combined cycle savings (360{,}399) in fact slightly \emph{exceed} the sum of the individual savings (307{,}450), because the NLP's extra mispredictions---and their flush cost---vanish under SGF.
Even combined, however, the 7-stage's 1.89\,CPI remains above the 6-stage's measured 1.83, so the 6-stage stays the throughput-optimal depth on this fabric; the IF2 bubble is real but only partially recoverable by a lightweight predictor.
A larger next-fetch table or a zero-bubble next-line-and-set predictor could recover more of the 0.28\,CPI bound at additional area cost; we leave that tradeoff to future work.

\subsection{FPGA vs.\ ASIC Depth Behavior}
\label{sec:fpga_asic}

The depth--frequency relationship on FPGA differs qualitatively from ASICs.
On ASICs, $F_{\max}$ increases roughly monotonically with depth because gate delay scales linearly with the number of logic levels per stage \cite{hrishikesh2002optimal}.
On FPGAs, LUT delay is function-independent (a 6-input LUT computes any Boolean function with the same propagation delay), and routing delay---which does not decrease with pipeline register insertion---dominates total path delay \cite{kuon2007measuring}.
This produces the two-tier structure observed here: only splits that break LUT-dominated logic chains (the execute stage) yield frequency gains; splits targeting routing-dominated paths (IF, MEM) do not.

The CPI model's per-stage costs (flush penalty, load-use stalls, IF2 bubble) are fabric-independent because they are driven by hazard structure, not physical timing.
Absolute throughput ($F_{\max}/\text{CPI}$) is fabric-specific.
This separation means the CPI model and SGF's misprediction reduction transfer to other FPGA families; only the frequency tier boundaries would shift.

\subsection{Anticipated Objections}
\label{sec:objections}

We address three objections directly, since each bears on how the results should be read.

\textbf{Is SGF general, or specific to this core?}
We claim generality for the \emph{mechanism}, not the magnitudes.
Mechanism~B arises whenever a global-history predictor's update latency exceeds zero---a structural property of in-order pipelines, not of Artix-7 or gshare in particular---and the bimodal control (no GHR, zero inflation) and the 32$\times$ PHT sweep establish this causally.
What transfers across designs is the Mechanism-A/B decomposition and the ROB-free dual-GHR construction; what does \emph{not} transfer without re-measurement is any specific percentage, which depends on the predictor size, the workload, and the fabric.
The SGF remedy is demonstrated on gshare; its extension to tournament/TAGE predictors is scoped as future work (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{Does a 31\% misprediction reduction matter?}
On its own it yields a modest 3\% CPI improvement on 7-stage CoreMark, because Mechanism~B is one of four CPI components (Section~\ref{sec:sgf_key}).
The case for SGF is not a large speedup but a favorable cost-benefit ratio: it removes an entire correctable error class at 0.5--0.7\% area with no frequency penalty, and pushes deep-pipeline accuracy below the shallow-pipeline baseline---an outcome unreachable by merely shortening the update path.
For a fixed-function embedded soft core shipped in volume, eliminating a nearly free-to-fix inefficiency is worthwhile even at single-digit CPI impact.

\textbf{How much depends on the benchmark set, \texttt{-O1}, and the absent memory hierarchy?}
The Mechanism-A depth trend holds on all seven workloads; Mechanism~B appears only on the two with short inter-branch distance and data-dependent outcomes, and the IBD heuristic (Table~\ref{tab:ibd}) predicts susceptibility on unseen workloads.
The \texttt{-O1} setting and single-cycle memory are stated boundaries (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}), and the cache-miss sensitivity analysis (Section~\ref{sec:cache_miss}) bounds how memory stalls dilute SGF's relative benefit.
We do not claim the optimal-depth conclusion is invariant to these factors---only that it is the measured outcome under the stated conditions on this fabric.

\subsection{Design Guidance for Practitioners}
\label{sec:guidance}

The data support the following guidance for RV32IM-class soft cores on Artix-7:
\begin{itemize}
\item The 6-stage pipeline is the highest-throughput depth in this configuration on this fabric. It captures the full frequency benefit of the execute-stage split while paying a moderate CPI penalty (64.1\,MIPS on CoreMark, 1.88 CoreMark/MHz).
\item The classic 5-stage RISC pipeline is a dominated design point on this fabric: it shares the 4-stage's frequency limitation while adding load-use stalls. Unless area is the binding constraint, the 5-stage offers no advantage. This dominance is conditional on the \texttt{-O1} workload mix measured here: higher optimization levels (loop unrolling, load scheduling) shift branch and load-use frequencies and could narrow or change the comparison.
\item The 7/8-stage designs match the 6-stage on frequency but pay progressively higher CPI costs. With SGF, the Mechanism~B component is eliminated, but Mechanism~A and IF2 bubble costs remain. These designs are justified when the IF split addresses a genuine timing bottleneck (e.g., synchronous BRAM instruction memory).
\item On branch-dense workloads, designers should implement SGF in any pipeline with 3 or more stages of predictor update latency---including the recommended 6-stage, where it cuts CoreMark mispredictions 23.9\% (Section~\ref{sec:sgf_6stage}). The 0.5--0.7\% area cost is negligible relative to the 17--31\% misprediction reduction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Causal Isolation of Mechanism~B}

The baseline measurements in Section~\ref{sec:mispred} established a correlation between pipeline depth and misprediction count on branch-dense workloads.
Two alternative explanations must be excluded before attributing this to GHR staleness: (1)~the inflation could be an aliasing artifact of the 64-entry PHT, resolvable by increasing table capacity; or (2)~some other pipeline-structural effect (e.g., forwarding interactions) could cause additional mispredictions at deeper depths independent of the predictor.
We design two controlled experiments to rule out each alternative.

\textbf{statemate (controlled result).}
We treat statemate as the load-bearing result of this experiment: its branch outcomes are data-dependent yet its branch pattern is sparse enough (IBD~=~8.4) that aliasing does not dominate, so the depth effect is observed against a stable baseline.
Across a 32$\times$ capacity range (PHT=32 to 1024), the misprediction inflation at 7/8-stage depth is +10.0\% at every PHT size from 32 to 256, with absolute counts varying by fewer than 8 mispredictions out of 73{,}000.
At PHT=512 and 1024 the larger table resolves some aliasing and lowers the absolute baseline, yet the depth-dependent inflation does not merely persist---it grows (+10.5\% and +11.8\%).
No PHT capacity from 32 to 1024 entries closes the depth gap, which is the expected signature of an effect that is not caused by table capacity: a 32$\times$ sweep cannot remove what aliasing did not create.

\textbf{CoreMark (high-aliasing corroboration).}
CoreMark has the densest branch pattern of any workload (IBD~=~4.0) and therefore the highest aliasing pressure, which makes its absolute baseline counts swing non-monotonically with table size (145K--172K; the 64-entry table happens to alias favorably, the 256-entry table unfavorably).
This aliasing noise partially masks the depth effect, so we deliberately do \emph{not} rest the causal claim on CoreMark's inflation magnitude.
Two depth-robust facts nonetheless survive the aliasing: the 6-stage exceeds the 4/5-stage baseline at \emph{every} one of the six table sizes, and the 7/8-stage exhibits a consistent +0.7--1.9\% inflation at every size.
The contrast between the two workloads is itself diagnostic.
The table-size-dependent baseline swing isolates the \emph{aliasing} axis, while the table-size-invariant depth gap isolates the \emph{staleness} axis; the two are orthogonal.
A larger table reduces absolute mispredictions but does not correct the stale-GHR inflation that SGF targets, and only the latter is the subject of this paper.

\textbf{Causal conclusion.}
These two experiments close the causal chain:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The bimodal control proves that Mechanism~B \emph{requires} a GHR---without one, no depth-dependent misprediction variation occurs on any workload.
\item The PHT capacity sweep proves that Mechanism~B is \emph{not} an aliasing artifact---the inflation persists at every table size from 32 to 1024 entries.
\item Therefore, Mechanism~B is caused specifically by stale GHR update latency in the pipeline, and SGF, which eliminates this latency, is the correct fix.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Power and Efficiency}
\label{sec:power}

Power is estimated with Vivado's \texttt{report\_power} on each out-of-context--synthesized variant, annotated with workload-specific switching activity captured as a SAIF file from a behavioral CoreMark simulation of that variant (Vivado reports ``Medium'' vector confidence).
Fig.~\ref{fig:power} shows the on-chip power breakdown.

\textbf{Energy-Delay Product (EDP).}
To evaluate the power-performance tradeoff, we compute the energy per CoreMark execution and the EDP:
\begin{equation}
E = P_{\text{dyn}} \times \frac{\text{Cycles}}{F_{\max}}; \quad \text{EDP} = E \times \frac{\text{Cycles}}{F_{\max}}
\end{equation}
From the workload-SAIF power and the measured CoreMark cycle counts, the 6-stage achieves the lowest energy per run (7.0\,mJ) and EDP (318\,mJ$\cdot$ms), versus 10.6\,mJ / 675\,mJ$\cdot$ms for the 4-stage and 8.0\,mJ / 432\,mJ$\cdot$ms for the 8-stage.
The 6-stage is therefore the most energy-efficient depth as well as the highest-throughput one---a conclusion that holds, and in fact sharpens, under workload-specific power.
SGF's additional 49 LUTs and 57 FFs add $<$1\% to dynamic power, confirming negligible energy overhead.

Fig.~\ref{fig:efficiency} shows power efficiency in MIPS/W (using $F_{\max}/\text{CPI}$ and total SAIF power).
The 6-stage leads at 286\,MIPS/W, ahead of the 7-stage (253) and 8-stage (248); the 4- and 5-stage trail at 195 and 197\,MIPS/W, their lower power outweighed by their lower frequency.
The 6-stage's efficiency lead is thus robust to the power methodology.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ====================================================================

The boundaries of this study constrain every conclusion drawn above and must be stated precisely.

\textbf{FPGA family dependence.}
All synthesis and timing data are from Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35TCPG236-1 using Vivado 2025.2.
The two-tier $F_{\max}$ structure (Section~\ref{sec:synth}) is a property of the 6-input LUT fabric and Vivado's placer/router; a different LUT architecture (e.g., Intel ALM with fracturable 8-input LUTs), routing topology, or process node could shift the execute-split frequency knee to a different pipeline depth or eliminate it entirely.
The absolute $F_{\max}$ values, area counts, and power figures do not transfer to other FPGA families without re-synthesis.

\textbf{Synthesis tool dependence.}
Vivado's out-of-context synthesis mode, placement directives, and timing-driven optimization heuristics affect both $F_{\max}$ and area.
A different tool (e.g., Intel Quartus, open-source Yosys+nextpnr) could produce different critical paths, different placement-dependent variance, and different area-frequency tradeoffs.
The 10-seed methodology mitigates single-seed bias within Vivado but does not address cross-tool variation.

\textbf{Single microarchitecture.}
The processor is a research vehicle, not a production-competitive core.
It lacks compressed-instruction (C extension) support, out-of-order execution, cache hierarchy beyond a single L1, TLB/MMU, and I/O subsystems.
Results characterize how \emph{this particular} microarchitecture responds to pipeline-depth changes.
A core with a different forwarding topology, a tournament predictor, superscalar issue, or a deeper memory hierarchy could produce a different optimal depth and different SGF impact.

\textbf{Memory hierarchy scope.}
All workloads fit within 16\,KB of instruction memory with near-100\% cache hit rates.
The cache miss sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:cache_miss} bounds SGF's benefit under higher miss rates, but the analysis is analytical (modeled), not measured with a real cache hierarchy.
Systems with external DDR or multi-level caches would need re-evaluation.

\textbf{CPI model per-workload parameters.}
The first-principles model (Eq.~\ref{eq:cpi_model}, $R^2 = 0.977$) decomposes CPI into physically meaningful terms but relies on per-workload $\gamma_w$ parameters that must be fitted empirically.
The model predicts the depth-dependent CPI components from architectural parameters alone; the workload-dependent base requires simulation or profiling.
The model has not been validated on workloads outside the seven used for fitting.

\textbf{SGF predictor scope and applicability to TAGE.}
SGF is demonstrated on a single-table gshare predictor, which is the appropriate design point for the resource-constrained FPGA soft cores this work targets: the 64-entry gshare occupies $<$1\% of the Artix-7 fabric (Section~\ref{sec:common}), whereas a multi-table TAGE predictor's storage and tag-comparison logic would consume a large fraction of a $\sim$7{,}500-LUT core and is rarely deployed in this class of design.
The stale-GHR effect that SGF corrects, however, is not specific to gshare: any predictor indexed by a global history register incurs it, and TAGE \cite{seznec2011tage}---which uses substantially longer histories across multiple tagged tables---is plausibly \emph{more} sensitive to update latency, though we do not measure this here.
SGF's contribution is therefore the \emph{ROB-free} realization of speculative history for in-order FPGA pipelines; high-performance out-of-order cores already obtain speculative history for TAGE via the reorder buffer, an option unavailable to a soft core that has no centralized checkpoint structure.
Extending SGF to TAGE would require a separate speculative history and a per-table checkpoint for each history length, and because stale history in a tagged predictor corrupts both the index and the tag---a qualitatively different error profile than gshare's index-only aliasing (Section~\ref{sec:background})---a measured TAGE evaluation is left to future work rather than asserted here.
Finally, SGF ships without the confidence filter we evaluated (Section~\ref{sec:crc32}), accepting a zero-CPI misprediction blip on crc32 rather than sacrificing CoreMark benefit; and SGF has not been tested with local-history (two-level adaptive \cite{yeh1991two}) or neural (perceptron \cite{jimenez2001dynamic}) predictors.

\textbf{Benchmark coverage.}
Seven workloads span synthetic (Dhrystone, Diagnostic), industry-standard (CoreMark), and embedded (four Embench-IoT) categories, but do not include floating-point, OS-level, or multi-threaded workloads.
The branch frequency range (4.4\%--26.3\%) and instruction counts (1.4K--4.5M) are representative of embedded applications but not of general-purpose computing.
Two of the seven workloads (CoreMark, statemate) exhibit measurable Mechanism~B behavior; the remaining five show zero depth-dependent misprediction variation.
The IBD-based classification in Table~\ref{tab:ibd} provides a practitioner heuristic for predicting Mechanism~B susceptibility on unseen workloads.

\textbf{Compiler optimization level.}
All benchmarks are compiled at \texttt{-O1}.
Higher optimization levels (\texttt{-O2}, \texttt{-O3}) may alter branch frequency, branch density, loop unrolling, and the correlation structure of global history, potentially affecting both the baseline misprediction rate and SGF's benefit.
The interaction between compiler optimization level and Mechanism~B susceptibility is an uncharacterized dimension of this study.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ====================================================================

This paper makes two contributions to the design of pipelined FPGA soft cores.
First, a controlled depth experiment---five RV32IM pipeline variants from shared RTL on Artix-7---separates depth-dependent CPI overhead into an inherent component (Mechanism~A: flush penalty scaling) and a correctable component (Mechanism~B: stale-GHR misprediction inflation of up to 10\%).
Causal isolation is established by two independent controls: a bimodal predictor (no GHR) produces identical misprediction counts across all five depths on the same workloads where gshare shows +5.7--10\% inflation, and a PHT capacity sweep from 32 to 1024 entries shows the inflation persists at every table size.
A first-principles CPI model ($R^2 = 0.977$, cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.941$) decomposes each cost into physically interpretable terms.

Second, Speculative GHR Forwarding (SGF) eliminates Mechanism~B by replacing the single committed GHR with a dual-GHR architecture that updates speculatively at prediction time and uses pipeline-register-forwarded checkpoints for rollback, requiring no reorder buffer.
SGF reduces mispredictions by 31.4\% on CoreMark and 22.7\% on statemate in 7-stage pipelines at 0.7\% area cost with no frequency degradation.
The zero-latency speculative update pushes deep-pipeline misprediction counts below the shallow-pipeline baseline, an effect not achievable by reducing update latency alone.
No workload shows CPI degradation; the lone misprediction blip (on crc32) is CPI-neutral.

The experiment also identifies the 6-stage pipeline as the highest-throughput design point on Artix-7, driven by a two-tier frequency structure ($p < 0.001$, Cohen's $d = 18.5$) whose sole knee is the execute-stage split.
This frequency result is specific to the 6-input LUT fabric and Vivado's placer; the optimal depth and the location of the knee may shift on other FPGA families or tools, so we present the 6-stage recommendation as fabric- and tool-conditional rather than universal.

For practitioners building gshare-class predictors on FPGA soft cores, SGF is a low-risk addition: an in-order pipeline with three or more stages of predictor update latency stands to benefit, at a negligible 0.5--0.7\% area cost and no frequency penalty, for a 17--31\% misprediction reduction on branch-dense workloads.
Whether the benefit carries to tournament/TAGE predictors, other FPGA fabrics, and richer memory hierarchies remains to be measured; what transfers without re-measurement is the Mechanism-A/B decomposition and the ROB-free dual-GHR construction, not the specific percentages.

Future work includes extending SGF to tournament and TAGE \cite{seznec2011tage} predictors---where the longer global histories may make stale-history inflation more severe, and each history length needs its own speculative state and checkpoint; validating the two-tier frequency structure on Intel FPGA fabrics (Cyclone~V, Arria~10); and evaluating SGF across compiler optimization levels to characterize the interaction between branch density and speculative history accuracy.

The complete RTL, testbenches, synthesis scripts, and benchmark programs are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/devanshjoshi08/RISCV-Pipeline-Research}.
